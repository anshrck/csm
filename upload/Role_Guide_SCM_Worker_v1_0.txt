1.  WHO YOU ARE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You are an SCM Worker — Service Customer Manager.

You sit in the Worker layer of the SeOS role model, within the Customer Service
Management (CSM) organizational function. CSM is the strategic front-office of
uSMS — the permanent, external-facing governance interface between the IT
organization and its Service Customers. Your role is the human embodiment of
that interface.

You are the named, personal contact for one or more Service Customers. You are
not a ticket processor. You are not a demand intake clerk. You are a governance
actor: the person who translates between the Service Customer's business language
and the IT organization's governance structures — and who ensures that every
interaction between those two parties is formal, traceable, and accountable.

Your primary function is governance, not relationship management as an end
in itself. The relationship exists to serve governance, not the other way around.
An SCM Worker who has a warm rapport with their Service Customer but delivers
vague quotes, loses communication threads during change execution, and ignores
SLA breach signals is not performing their role — regardless of how the
customer feels about them personally.

Your three process domains within CSM:

  Demand Management
    The end-to-end lifecycle process for service change demand. Every request
    from a Service Customer to create, modify, or retire a service enters the
    system through you and is governed by you until fulfillment.

  Service Catalog Management
    The ongoing accuracy governance of the service catalog as it applies to
    your Service Customers. You identify when catalog changes are needed,
    trigger the change process, and verify the result.

  Service Level Management
    The continuous monitoring and communication of SLA performance for your
    Service Customers. You translate performance data into customer-relevant
    communication and ensure that breaches are never silent.

These three are not separate jobs. They are three dimensions of the same
responsibility: ensuring that the IT organization's commitment to your Service
Customers is established, accurate, and honored.

2.  YOUR POSITION IN THE GOVERNANCE ARCHITECTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The SeOS governance chain:
  CUSTOMER → DEMAND → QUOTE → SERVICE → EXECUTION → OUTCOME → EXPERIENCE

You govern the first three steps: you receive the Customer, assess the Demand,
and produce the Quote. You then maintain communication ownership through
Execution and confirm the Outcome.

SeOS role model — your position:
  Owner layer:    Process Owner (CM), Service Owner — governance and escalation
  Leader layer:   CM Leader — your operational authority
  Worker layer:   SCM Worker — you. Primary executor of all CSM processes.

You are the only role in the system that maintains the complete customer
relationship thread from demand submission through fulfillment. Every other
role touches the demand at a specific point. You are there from the first
conversation to the closed record.

Inheritance chain:
  HRX → SeOS → uSMS → uSeC

Every decision you make as an SCM Worker must pass two compliance checks:

  HRX compliance: Does this decision preserve coherence for the Service
    Customer? Does it fragment their attention, create invisible pressure, or
    consume their cognitive bandwidth without producing value? If yes — the
    decision is non-compliant.

  SeOS coherence: Is every responsibility in this interaction explicit? Is
    every service interaction governed? Does structure precede execution?
    If not — the interaction is incomplete.

The Separation Principle:
  uSeC describes what is offered. uSMS governs how it is delivered.
  CSM triggers and oversees catalog changes. Change Enablement executes them.
  You never modify the service catalog directly — not even when the change
  is simple, obvious, or urgent. This boundary is structural, not procedural.

3.  THE CUSTOMERS YOU SERVE — AND WHAT THAT MEANS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You are assigned to one or more Service Customers. Each Service Customer is an
organizational unit — a business division, a department, or an external company
— that has a formal service agreement with the IT organization.

What your assignment means:

  Named relationship, not anonymous intake
    Your Service Customer knows who you are. When they have a demand, they
    contact you. When something goes wrong with their services, you call them.
    When there is a breach, you do not send an automated notification — you
    provide governed communication with context.

  Complete institutional memory
    You carry the demand history, commitment record, and SLA performance data
    for every Service Customer assigned to you. You know what was promised,
    what was delivered, and where the gaps are. You are the organizational
    memory of the customer relationship — not a tool that stores it.

  Accountability without delegation of accountability
    You may delegate tasks — preparation of a quote, drafting of a report —
    but you do not delegate accountability. If communication with a Service
    Customer fails, if a demand falls through the cracks, if an SLA breach is
    not communicated promptly, the accountability sits with you and escalates
    to your CM Leader. Not to the system. Not to another team.

The Parent-Child Principle in your work:
  Every Service User you encounter belongs to a Service Customer. Service Users
  cannot submit demands. Service Users cannot negotiate service agreements.
  If a Service User contacts you asking for something that belongs in Demand
  Management, you redirect them to their Service Customer — who may then
  contact you with a formal demand. This is not procedural bureaucracy. It
  is the structural boundary that protects both the Service User and the IT
  organization from unstructured demand entering through the wrong channel.

In a B2B context — where your Service Customer is an external organization —
your role takes on commercial significance. You are not just the governance
contact. You are the primary relationship owner for a paying client. The
governance discipline of your role — honest assessment, realistic quotes, SLA
accountability — becomes a direct driver of client satisfaction and retention.

4.  DEMAND MANAGEMENT: YOUR CORE WORK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Demand Management is the end-to-end lifecycle governance of service change
demand. Every request to create, modify, or retire a service enters the system
through this process — and you are its primary executor.

State machine:
  NEW → UNDER_REVIEW → QUOTED → ACCEPTED → IN_CHANGE → FULFILLED → CLOSED
                     → REJECTED   → CLOSED
                     → REDIRECTED → CLOSED

4.1  Receive and Create the Demand Record

When a Service Customer contacts you with a demand, you create a Demand Record
in the system. The Service Customer must be a legitimate actor within their
organizational scope (BR-CM-001). If a Service User contacts you directly with
what they believe is a demand, you redirect them to their Service Customer.

The Demand Record is the governance artifact that makes everything else
traceable. It is not an email thread. It is not a meeting note. It is the
formal record from which the entire demand lifecycle runs.

Status: NEW. DemandCreated domain event emitted.

4.2  Assess: The Catalog Check First

Before anything else: check the service catalog. Does the demand describe
something that already exists as an active service or request type?

If yes: this demand is a Redirect. The Service Customer does not need a new
service — they need access to or guidance about an existing one. You inform
them and close the demand as REDIRECTED. This is not a failure of their demand.
It is the system working correctly.

If no: the demand is feasible or requires evaluation. You move to full
assessment: technical and portfolio feasibility, historical demand data for
estimation reference, Service Owner consultation where required.

Status: UNDER_REVIEW. DemandUnderReview event emitted.

The catalog check is not a formality. It is the primary governance function of
the assessment step. An SCM Worker who skips it and quotes a new service when
an existing one would serve is creating unnecessary cost, complexity, and
catalog bloat. The catalog check is the first act of demand governance.

4.3  Determine the Outcome: Redirect, Reject, or Quote

After assessment, every demand resolves to one of three outcomes:

  Redirect: Already covered. Inform the customer. Close as REDIRECTED.

  Reject: Not feasible, out of scope, or submitted by a non-legitimate actor.
    Document the rejection reason (mandatory — BR-CM-004). CM Leader approves
    the rejection. Communicate to the Service Customer. Close as REJECTED.
    A rejection must be explained. Never close a demand as rejected without a
    documented, customer-facing reason.

  Quote: Feasible and not covered. Proceed to quote preparation.

  Deferred: Cannot be assessed or committed to immediately (budget cycle
    dependency, strategic decision pending). Remain in UNDER_REVIEW with a
    documented reason and a scheduled review date. Not a terminal state.

4.4  Issue the Quote

When a demand warrants a quote, you prepare the preliminary assessment:
  – Scope: what will be delivered, in what form
  – Estimated effort (days) and timeline
  – SLA class that will govern the resulting service
  – Cost range or resource implications (if applicable)
  – Any dependencies or constraints

Critical rule: your CM Leader approves every quote before it is issued to the
Service Customer. You do not issue quotes unilaterally. This is not a
bureaucratic step — it is the governance gate that ensures the IT organization
makes commitments it can actually keep. A quote that has not been approved by
the CM Leader is not a commitment. If you issue one, you have created a promise
without governance backing.

Status: QUOTED. DemandQuoted event emitted.

4.5  Record Acceptance Formally

When the Service Customer approves the quote, you record their acceptance
formally in the system (BR-CM-002). Verbal acceptance is not sufficient. The
acceptance transitions the demand to the ACCEPTED state and commits both parties:
the IT organization to delivering what was quoted, the Service Customer to the
associated implications.

From the ACCEPTED transition, the SLA clock of the commitment begins to apply.

Status: ACCEPTED. DemandAccepted event emitted.

4.6  Hand Over to Change Enablement

With the demand accepted, you create a Change Request in Change Enablement
and submit a formal ProcessHandover record (type: CM_TO_CE). This handover
record is:
  – Immutable and timestamped
  – Carrying the demand reference, assessment context, and customer scope
  – The trigger by which Change Enablement assumes execution responsibility

The CE Worker acknowledges receipt of the handover (BR-HO-001). From this
acknowledgement, Change Enablement owns execution. You do not own execution.
You own the customer relationship — which means you own communication.

Status: IN_CHANGE. DemandInChange event emitted.

4.7  Retain Communication Ownership Through Execution

This is the rule that distinguishes a well-run CSM function from a poorly
run one. From the moment the demand transitions to IN_CHANGE, the Service
Customer interacts with you — not with the CE Worker, not with the CE Leader,
not with the Technical Owner. You.

The CE Worker reports progress to you. You communicate that progress to the
Service Customer. You track milestones. You communicate delays. You communicate
completion. The Service Customer never has to wonder who to call when they want
to know the status of their demand.

This is BR-HO-001: the originating process retains external communication
responsibility until the receiving process confirms completion. In practical
terms: until the change is done and the catalog is updated, you speak for the
IT organization to your Service Customer.

An SCM Worker who considers their work done at the IN_CHANGE transition is
failing their role. The transition marks the beginning of a delivery phase
during which your Service Customer needs you most.

4.8  Confirm Fulfillment and Close

When Change Enablement confirms completion and the service catalog has been
updated to reflect the new reality, you verify that what was delivered matches
the committed scope. You then confirm fulfillment to the Service Customer and
formally close the Demand Record.

Status: FULFILLED → CLOSED. DemandFulfilled and DemandClosed events emitted.

The demand is not FULFILLED until:
  – The change is technically complete
  – The service catalog reflects the new service state
  – You have verified the catalog entry against the committed scope
  – You have communicated completion to the Service Customer

A Demand Record that remains open after delivery is complete is a governance
debt. Close it.

5.  SERVICE CATALOG MANAGEMENT: KEEPING REALITY ACCURATE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The service catalog is the governance record of what the IT organization has
committed to deliver. It must at all times reflect reality — not an aspirational
state, not a historical state, but the current, accurate state of services and
commitments.

Your role in Service Catalog Management:

  Monitor catalog accuracy for your Service Customers continuously
    Does the catalog reflect what was committed to them? Are services that
    were modified still showing the old scope? Are retired services still
    appearing? Are SLA class assignments aligned with the current commitments?
    You are the person most qualified to catch these discrepancies — because
    you know what was promised.

  Identify when a catalog change is needed
    Three types of catalog changes exist: Addition (new service after a demand
    is fulfilled), Modification (scope, SLA class, or offering changes),
    Retirement (service no longer delivered). You identify the need. You
    document what needs to change and why.

  Create the Change Request and submit to Change Enablement
    You do not edit the catalog directly. You create a Normal Change Request in
    Change Enablement with the catalog change scope documented. Your CM Leader
    approves it. You submit a CM_TO_CE ProcessHandover record.

  Communicate to affected Service Customers before and after
    For modifications and retirements: inform the Service Customer before the
    change is executed. For retirements: obtain their explicit confirmation
    before triggering the retirement Change Request. No service may be retired
    without customer acknowledgement. For additions: notify the customer when
    the new service is live.

  Verify the catalog entry after Change Enablement confirms completion
    Check that the service name, description, uSeC classification, SLA class,
    request types, Service Owner, and Technical Owner assignments are all
    correct. Discrepancies go back to CE for correction before you close the
    loop.

Minimum cadence: a formal catalog accuracy review for each of your Service
Customers at least quarterly. Not a courtesy check — a structured review of
every active catalog entry against the current committed state.

6.  SERVICE LEVEL MANAGEMENT: GOVERNING COMMITMENTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLM is not a reporting function. It is a governance function. Your job is not
to produce numbers — it is to ensure that SLA commitments are visible, that
approaching problems are communicated before they become breaches, and that
when breaches occur they are handled as governance events, not embarrassments.

Your SLM responsibilities:

  Establish and confirm the SLA profile at demand acceptance
    When a demand is accepted, you confirm the SLA class for each affected
    service — ensuring it is consistent with the service's catalog assignment.
    You consult the CM Leader on the SLA class if the demand involves a new
    service or a significant scope change. The SLA class is set once and
    monitored continuously from that point.

  Monitor SLA performance for your Service Customers
    At the Minimal Stage: periodic SlaEvent queries and SLM dashboard review.
    At the Smart Stage: real-time performance data through the Experience Layer.
    You are the first human in the chain to see when performance is degrading.
    Do not wait for a breach to act.

  Communicate SLA warnings proactively
    When the SLA monitoring system emits an SLAWarning event (at 80% of the
    clock consumed), you contact your Service Customer proactively. Not after
    the breach. Before it. The warning exists precisely so that intervention
    is still possible. An SCM Worker who receives a warning and waits to see
    whether a breach actually occurs has failed the governance obligation of
    their role.

  Communicate breaches with context
    When a breach occurs, your Service Customer receives governed communication:
    what happened, which service, what the current status is, and what
    remediation is planned. Not just a breach notification. A communication
    that respects their right to understand what they are experiencing.

    The Service Owner is also notified at breach. Your CM Leader confirms the
    breach and escalates. You communicate to the Service Customer throughout.

  Produce periodic SLA performance reports
    Regular reports per Service Customer, per service: compliance rates, breach
    history, remediation status. These reports must be accurate and complete
    before they reach the customer. Your CM Leader reviews them before issuance.

  Identify SLA class misalignments
    When monitoring data reveals that a service is consistently performing
    outside the range of its assigned SLA class — either over-committing (Class
    A behavior from a Class C service) or under-delivering (Class C behavior
    from a Class B commitment) — you flag this for catalog modification.

A breach is a governance moment. Not a failure to hide. Every breach that is
communicated clearly, with context and a credible remediation plan, builds more
trust than a service that never breaks but is governed opaquely.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7.  COMMUNICATION OWNERSHIP — THE RULE YOU NEVER BREAK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This section exists because communication ownership is the single most
frequently violated principle in customer governance roles — and the one whose
violation has the most immediate, visible impact on customer trust.

The rule: from the moment a demand is submitted to the moment it is closed, you
maintain external communication responsibility toward the Service Customer.
This responsibility does not transfer when the demand enters Change Enablement.
It does not pause when technical complexity increases. It does not end when
the topic becomes uncomfortable.

What this looks like in practice:

  At every transition, you communicate.
    NEW → UNDER_REVIEW: "I have received your demand and am beginning
    assessment. I will come back to you with an outcome within [timeframe]."
    QUOTED: "I have a quote ready for your review." Not an automated email.
    You.

  During execution, you communicate status at meaningful milestones.
    Not every day. Not a system notification. A human communication when
    something material has happened or when the timeline needs to be updated.

  When there are delays, you communicate first.
    The Service Customer should never contact you to ask why they have not
    heard anything. You tell them before they ask.

  When something changes in the scope or timeline, you communicate it as a
  governed update — with a reason, a revised expectation, and your name
  on it.

  When SLA performance is degrading, you communicate it before it breaches.
    Not when the breach notification is automatically generated. Before.

The Service Customer's trust in the IT organization is built or destroyed
through you. Every communication you make or fail to make is a data point
in their assessment of whether the governance relationship is real or
cosmetic. Make it real.

8.  YOUR AUTHORITIES AND YOUR LIMITS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What you are authorized to do:

  – Create Demand Records on behalf of Service Customers
  – Conduct demand assessment and determine outcome type (Redirect, Reject,
    Quote) — subject to CM Leader approval on quotes and rejections
  – Prepare quotes for CM Leader review and approval
  – Record formal customer acceptance of quotes
  – Submit ProcessHandover records to Change Enablement
  – Maintain communication with Service Customers throughout demand lifecycle
  – Identify catalog change needs and create Change Requests (with CM Leader
    approval) for submission to CE
  – Monitor SLA performance data for your Service Customers
  – Issue governed SLA communications to your Service Customers
  – Produce SLA performance reports (subject to CM Leader review before
    issuance)

What you are not authorized to do:

  You cannot issue quotes without CM Leader approval.
    Every quote is a commitment from the IT organization. You do not have
    unilateral authority to make organizational commitments. The CM Leader
    approval gate is structural — not a bureaucratic delay.

  You cannot modify the service catalog directly.
    Only Change Enablement may write to the catalog. This is the Separation
    Principle (BR-CE-004). No matter how simple or obvious the catalog change
    appears, it goes through CE.

  You cannot communicate with Technical Owners directly about delivery.
    Technical communication about change execution flows through Change
    Enablement. You communicate with the CE Worker about progress. Not with
    Technical Owners. Not with engineers.

  You cannot make commitments on behalf of the CM Leader or Service Owner.
    Your authority is to prepare, assess, and communicate. Governance decisions
    at the commitment level require the approval of the people authorized to
    make them.

  You cannot serve Service Users in the CSM channel.
    Service Users belong in the Service Desk. If a Service User contacts you,
    redirect them appropriately. They are not your actor group.

  You cannot also be the CE Leader for the same service scope.
    Separation of duties: the person who assesses a demand and issues a quote
    must not be the same person who approves the resulting change (DAL-16,
    Section 7). This is structural — not a staffing preference.

9.  YOUR RELATIONSHIP TO THE CM LEADER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your CM Leader is the operational authority for the entire CSM function. They
hold accountability for throughput, assessment quality, and communication
standards across all SCM Workers. They are not a supervisor in the traditional
sense — they are the governance authority above you in the CSM function.

What the CM Leader does for you:

  Approves quotes before issuance
    You prepare. They validate. No quote leaves CSM without their approval.

  Approves rejection decisions where rejection is contested
    If a Service Customer disputes a rejection, the CM Leader's authority is
    the next governance level. You document; they authorize.

  Escalates to the Service Owner when required
    Commitment disputes, significant SLA breaches, and governance escalations
    that go beyond the SCM Worker level move through the CM Leader to the
    Service Owner.

  Reviews SLA performance reports before they are issued
    Your reports are accurate before the CM Leader sees them. Their review
    ensures completeness and consistency before the Service Customer receives them.

  Monitors the demand pipeline across all SCM Workers
    The CM Leader has visibility into every active demand, its status, and its
    communication health. If your demand is stalling — in UNDER_REVIEW too
    long, IN_CHANGE without communication updates — the CM Leader will ask.

What you do for the CM Leader:

  You are the quality of CSM, made visible.
    The CM Leader's accountability is yours in practice. Your assessment quality,
    your communication discipline, your quote accuracy — these are what the
    CM Leader is held accountable for. Poor discipline in your work is poor
    discipline in the function they lead.

  You surface problems early.
    When a demand is going badly — when a change is delayed, when a Service
    Customer is dissatisfied, when an SLA breach is likely — you tell the CM
    Leader before it becomes a crisis. The CM Leader needs to know what is
    happening before escalation lands on their desk from an external source.

10. WORKING WITH CHANGE ENABLEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Change Enablement (CE) is your execution partner. You trigger changes. They
execute them. You do not execute. They do not communicate with your Service
Customers. This division of responsibilities is structural and mutual.

Your obligations toward CE:

  Submit complete, accurate ProcessHandover records
    The CE Worker picks up your handover and begins assessment from what you
    provide. A handover that is incomplete, ambiguous, or missing customer
    context forces CE to start their work with a gap you created. The demand
    record, the accepted quote scope, and the customer context must be in the
    handover before you submit it.

  Be available for CE queries during assessment and planning
    CE may need to clarify scope or customer expectations before they can plan
    the change. You are the channel to the Service Customer. Respond promptly.

  Track CE progress and communicate to your Service Customer
    CE reports milestones to you. You communicate them to the Service Customer.
    If CE is delayed, you communicate that. If CE encounters unexpected
    complexity, you communicate that. The Service Customer does not hear about
    change execution from CE directly.

  Verify the catalog upon CE completion
    When CE confirms the change is complete, you verify the catalog entry
    reflects the committed scope before closing the demand.

CE's obligations toward you:

  Acknowledge the ProcessHandover record (BR-HO-001)
    CE assumes execution responsibility from this acknowledgement.

  Report progress at defined milestones
    CE communicates status to you — not to your Service Customer — at key
    points in the change lifecycle.

  Confirm completion
    CE confirms to you when the change is complete and the catalog is updated.
    This is your trigger to verify and confirm fulfillment to the Service
    Customer.

If CE and CSM disagree about scope or delivery, the resolution path is:
SCM Worker ↔ CE Worker → CM Leader ↔ CE Leader → Service Owner (if required).

11. WORKING WITH PROBLEM MANAGEMENT AND SLA BREACHES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You are not a participant in Problem Management. You are a recipient of its
outputs — specifically when Problem Management's findings affect SLA commitments
for your Service Customers.

What Problem Management produces that is relevant to you:

  Root cause findings for SLA breach patterns
    When Problem Management completes its investigation into a recurring
    failure, the findings — root cause, decision (fix / Known Error / accept),
    and remediation path — feed into your SLA governance communication. When
    you communicate an SLA breach to your Service Customer, you provide this
    context. Not just "there was a breach," but "here is what was investigated,
    here is the decision, and here is what it means for the service going forward."

  Change triggers that affect your catalog
    When Problem Management decides a structural fix is warranted, it triggers
    a change in Change Enablement. That change may affect catalog entries in
    your customers' portfolios. You communicate this — before the change
    executes if possible, certainly after.

  Known Error resolutions
    When a Known Error is resolved (the underlying problem is fixed via a
    change), the Known Error record is closed. If the Known Error was associated
    with a service in your customer's portfolio, you communicate the resolution:
    the issue is structurally fixed, not just worked around.

Your role in breach communication is irreplaceable:
  Problem Management produces the root cause and decision.
  Service Level Management produces the performance data.
  You translate both into a customer communication that is honest, timely,
  and governed. A breach that reaches your Service Customer through an
  automated system notification, without context or human communication from
  you, is a governance failure in the customer relationship. Not a process
  step. A relationship failure.

12. COGNI: YOUR OPERATIONAL INTELLIGENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Cogni is available to you as an operational intelligence layer within the
Experience Layer. It is governed by the same entitlement boundaries as
everything else — your view is scoped to your Service Customer assignments.

What Cogni does for you as an SCM Worker:

  Smart Stage
    – Demand estimation assistance: based on historical demand data for similar
      requests, Cogni surfaces estimated effort ranges for your assessment.
      This is advisory — your judgment governs the quote. Cogni reduces
      the lookup burden, not the governance responsibility.
    – SLA performance at a glance: a dashboard of SLA status across your
      Service Customers, surfacing warnings and breach risks before you would
      otherwise notice them.
    – Communication drafting: after an SLA breach event, Cogni drafts a
      structured customer communication incorporating Problem Management root
      cause data — for you to review, adjust, and send. You do not send
      AI-generated communications without review. Accountability is yours.
    – Demand pipeline visibility: status of all active Demand Records assigned
      to you, with time-in-state flags for records that may be stalling.

  Mature Stage
    – Demand forecasting: based on your Service Customers' usage patterns and
      demand history, Cogni identifies likely upcoming demands — allowing you
      to be proactive rather than reactive.
    – SLA class recommendation: when monitoring data suggests a service's
      actual criticality has shifted, Cogni surfaces the analysis for your
      discussion with the customer and the CM Leader.

What Cogni never does:
  – Issue quotes or make commitments on your behalf
  – Communicate with Service Customers without your review
  – Surface another customer's data within your view
  – Replace the CM Leader approval step for quotes


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
13. WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

An SCM Worker is performing their role well when:

  Every active demand has a documented, current status.
    No demand sits in UNDER_REVIEW or IN_CHANGE without a recent communication
    record. No demand is open past fulfillment.

  Quotes are accurate.
    The IT organization delivers what it quoted. When scope must change, the
    Service Customer is told before the change happens, not after.

  Communication is ahead of the Service Customer.
    Your customers do not contact you to ask for status. You have already told
    them. This is not a matter of sending more messages — it is a matter of
    timing communication at the right moments, with the right content.

  SLA warnings are never silent.
    Every SLAWarning event for your customers results in a proactive
    communication from you. Before the breach.

  Breach communications contain context.
    When a breach occurs, your Service Customer understands what happened, what
    is being done, and what it means for their services. Not just a timestamp.

  Your catalog entries reflect current reality.
    When you verify a catalog entry after a change, you actually verify it.
    Discrepancies are returned to CE for correction before you close the loop.

  You surface problems to your CM Leader before they become external issues.
    The CM Leader never hears about a demand problem from the Service Customer
    before hearing it from you.

The standard is not perfection. The standard is governed accountability.
Problems happen. What distinguishes a well-run CSM function is not the absence
of problems — it is the presence of governance when problems occur.
