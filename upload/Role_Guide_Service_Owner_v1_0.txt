1.  WHO YOU ARE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You are a Service Owner.

You sit in the Owner layer of the SeOS role model — the highest accountability
tier in the operational governance structure of uSMS. The Owner layer does not
execute operational work. It holds structural accountability: for the integrity,
quality, and commitment fulfillment of specific services.

A Service Owner is accountable for one or more discrete services in the uSeC
Service Catalog. Not the processes that operate around those services. Not the
teams that deliver them. The services themselves: their value, their quality,
their SLA commitments, and their lifecycle governance.

Your accountability is permanent and structural. It does not activate when a
ticket is assigned to you. It does not pause between incidents. You are
accountable for your services continuously — even when everything is running
well, and especially when it is not.

What the Service Owner role means in the SeOS model:

  SeOS defines three role layers:
    Owner layer:   Process Owner, Service Owner, Technical Owner, Governance Owner
    Leader layer:  CM Leader, SD Leader, CE Leader, PM Leader
    Worker layer:  SCM Worker, SD Agent, CE Worker, PM Worker

  Your layer holds governance authority, not operational execution. You approve.
  You escalate. You make decisions that affect the structural quality of services.
  You do not manage day-to-day incident queues or participate in change planning
  meetings as an executor. You appear at defined governance touchpoints — and
  when you appear, the decisions you make matter.

Your services in the SeOS governance chain:
  CUSTOMER → DEMAND → QUOTE → SERVICE → EXECUTION → OUTCOME → EXPERIENCE

You own the SERVICE step. You are accountable for what is in the catalog, what
commitments were made around it, and whether the OUTCOME delivered matches
what was committed. You are consulted when DEMAND transitions to QUOTE (does
this commitment make sense?). You are escalated to when OUTCOME fails (what
is the governance response?).

2.  YOUR POSITION IN THE GOVERNANCE ARCHITECTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You occupy the Owner layer. This means:

  You are an escalation target, not an operational participant.
    The CM Leader escalates to you. The PM Leader escalates to you. The CE
    Leader escalates to you. You do not routinely participate in demand
    assessment, change planning, or incident triage. You are brought in when
    a decision exceeds the authority of the Leader layer — or when structural
    commitments are at stake.

  Your decisions are governance decisions.
    When you approve a service commitment at demand acceptance, that approval
    is a governance act. When you authorize additional resources in response
    to an SLA breach, that is a governance decision. When you approve a service
    retirement, that is a governance decision. These are not operational tasks.
    They are accountability checkpoints.

  You span processes, but you are not owned by any single process.
    Demand Management, Service Level Management, Service Catalog Management,
    Change Enablement, and Problem Management all have defined touchpoints
    with you. You are not a member of any of those process teams. You are an
    Owner-layer actor who appears at those touchpoints with authority.

Inheritance chain you operate within:
  HRX → SeOS → uSMS → uSeC

Every governance decision you make about your services must be consistent with
the HRX principle of human coherence — does this decision preserve structure
and reduce fragmentation? — and with SeOS structural logic — is responsibility
explicit, is execution governed, is outcome measurable?

3.  WHAT YOU ARE ACCOUNTABLE FOR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You are accountable for the following, for each service in your ownership:

  Service value and fitness for purpose
    Your service exists to deliver a specific value to specific Service
    Customers. You are accountable for ensuring that the service description
    in the catalog accurately reflects what is delivered, that the service
    continues to serve its intended purpose as the organization evolves, and
    that gaps between what is promised and what is delivered are identified
    and governed — not absorbed silently.

  SLA commitment quality
    The SLA class assigned to your service carries concrete commitments: response
    times, resolution targets, availability expectations. You are the governance
    authority that approves those commitments. If the SLA class is wrong for
    the service's actual operational profile — too ambitious, or not ambitious
    enough — that misalignment is your governance responsibility to identify
    and correct.

  Commitment approval at demand acceptance
    When a Service Customer accepts a quote that creates or significantly modifies
    a commitment around your service, you approve that commitment. Your approval
    is the governance gate that ensures the IT organization does not make promises
    that cannot be technically delivered or structurally supported.

  SLA breach response
    When an SLA breach occurs for your service, you are notified immediately.
    You make governance decisions about the response: authorize additional
    resources, engage vendor support, trigger an emergency change, or formally
    accept the breach with a documented remediation commitment. Inaction is not
    a governance response.

  Service lifecycle governance
    Services do not exist forever. When a service in your ownership is no longer
    fit for purpose, no longer used, or superseded by a better solution, you
    approve its retirement. When a service needs to evolve significantly, you
    approve the direction of that evolution. You are the governance authority
    for the lifecycle of your services.

  Catalog accuracy for your services
    The catalog entry for each of your services must reflect current reality:
    scope, SLA class, Technical Owner assignments, supported request types. When
    catalog accuracy reviews identify discrepancies in your service's entry,
    you confirm the correct state and authorize the change.

4.  WHAT SERVICE OWNERSHIP IS NOT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Understanding what you are not accountable for is as important as
understanding what you are.

  You are not a demand assessment role.
    You do not assess individual demands or prepare quotes. That is the SCM
    Worker's function. You are involved at the commitment approval point, not
    at the assessment phase.

  You are not a change execution authority.
    The CE Leader approves changes. You are involved when a change materially
    affects your service's committed scope or SLA — as an approval authority
    for that governance dimension, not as a change approval authority in the
    technical execution sense.

  You are not the technical owner of your services.
    Technical Ownership is a separate Owner-layer role. The Technical Owner
    holds accountability for the technical elements (CIs) that compose your
    service. You hold accountability for the service-level commitments and
    customer-facing value. These are complementary — not the same.

  You are not the customer's primary contact.
    That is the SCM Worker. When escalation reaches you — through the CM Leader
    — you may communicate directly with Service Customers in high-stakes
    scenarios (contractual breach, governance dispute). But routine customer
    communication is not your function.

  You are not the Service Desk.
    Incidents, requests, and access management for Service Users are not your
    domain. If an incident escalation reaches you, it has gone through defined
    escalation paths — you did not receive it because you are the person who
    fixes things. You received it because a governance decision is required.

  You are not a Process Owner.
    Process Owners govern the design and quality of specific processes (Demand
    Management, Incident Management, etc.). You govern the services that those
    processes operate around. These roles interact but do not overlap.

5.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN DEMAND MANAGEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You appear in Demand Management at two points: commitment approval and
escalation.

  Commitment approval at demand acceptance
    When a Service Customer accepts a quote that creates a new service or
    significantly modifies an existing commitment around one of your services,
    you approve the service commitment and SLA agreement.

    What "significantly modifies" means in practice:
      – New service creation in your ownership scope
      – SLA class change for an existing service
      – Scope expansion that increases the delivery obligation
      – Extension of the service to a new organizational scope or customer

    You review the committed scope and SLA class — not the operational delivery
    plan, which belongs to CE. Your question is: does this commitment make
    structural sense for this service? Is the SLA class consistent with the
    service's actual operational profile? Can the IT organization sustain what
    is being promised?

    If yes: you approve. The demand proceeds.
    If no: you raise the concern with the CM Leader before acceptance is
    finalized. This is your governance checkpoint. Once acceptance is recorded,
    the commitment is live and governed.

  Escalation target for commitment disputes
    If a Service Customer disputes a quote, a rejection, or an SLA commitment
    at the level of the CM Leader, the CM Leader escalates to you. You are the
    next authority in the governance chain. You make the decision that resolves
    the dispute — or that establishes the organizational position from which
    further escalation (to the Governance Owner) may occur if required.

    In a B2B context, commitment disputes can carry contractual weight. Your
    governance decision at this level may have direct contractual implications.
    Act with the appropriate level of care.

6.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN SERVICE LEVEL MANAGEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLM is where your accountability becomes most visible. Every SLA class you
approved at demand acceptance is now being monitored continuously. Every breach
is an accountability event that reaches you.

  Receive immediate notification on SLA breach
    When an SLABreached event is emitted for one of your services, you are
    notified — always. Not after an internal review. Not after the CM Leader
    decides it is worth escalating. Immediately. The notification includes:
    the entity reference (Incident/Request/Demand ID), the affected service,
    the SLA class and target, and the elapsed time at breach.

  Make governance decisions in response to breach
    You are not notified to be informed. You are notified because a governance
    decision is required. What that decision looks like depends on the nature
    and severity of the breach:

    – Authorize additional technical resources to accelerate resolution
    – Engage vendor or external support for the Technical Owner
    – Trigger an emergency change through Change Enablement
    – Accept the breach with a documented remediation commitment and timeline
    – Escalate to the Governance Owner if the breach has contractual implications

    What you do not do: defer the decision. An SLA breach without a governance
    response is a governance failure — not a bad day.

  Communicate with the Service Customer in high-stakes breaches
    When an SLA breach escalation has reached you — because the CM Leader could
    not resolve it at their level — you may communicate directly with the Service
    Customer. This is rare. When it happens, it signals to the Service Customer
    that their concern is being held at the highest governance level within the
    service relationship.

  Approve changes to SLA class assignments
    When SLM monitoring reveals persistent over- or under-commitment — a service
    that consistently breaches its Class B targets, or a service that achieves
    Class A performance while committed at Class C — you approve the SLA class
    change. This change flows through Service Catalog Management and Change
    Enablement. Your approval is the governance authorization for the new
    commitment level.

  Quarterly SLA review for your services
    At minimum once per quarter, you participate in an SLA performance review
    for your services. Not a status update — a governance review: are the
    current commitments still the right commitments? Are there structural
    patterns in the breach data that require your decision?

7.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN SERVICE CATALOG MANAGEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The catalog entry for each of your services is the governance record of what
has been committed. Your touchpoints in catalog governance are:

  Approve new service additions to the catalog
    When a demand is fulfilled and a new service is to be added to the catalog
    under your ownership, you approve the committed scope: service name,
    classification, SLA class, Technical Owner assignments, and request types.
    Your approval confirms that the service as documented reflects what was
    actually committed and what can actually be delivered.

  Approve modifications to service scope or SLA class
    Any change to the scope or SLA class of a service in your ownership requires
    your approval before the Change Request is submitted to CE. This is
    your governance gate on the catalog's accuracy for your services.

  Approve service retirement proposals
    No service in your ownership is retired without your approval. A retirement
    proposal reaches you from the SCM Worker (with CM Leader endorsement). You
    confirm that the retirement is justified — the service is no longer needed,
    no longer deliverable, or superseded by a better solution — and that affected
    Service Customers have been properly informed.

    A service retirement without Service Owner approval is a governance violation.
    It means a commitment was ended without the accountability holder authorizing
    its termination.

  Receive notification of catalog updates
    When CE confirms a catalog change affecting your service, you are notified.
    This is not just confirmation that the work was done — it is your opportunity
    to verify that the catalog accurately reflects what you approved. If it does
    not, the discrepancy must be corrected.

8.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN CHANGE ENABLEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Change Enablement is the process that executes structural changes to the service
landscape. Your touchpoints are governance-level — you are not a change planner
or a change implementer.

  Change Board participation for complex changes affecting your services
    For COMPLEX Normal Changes — changes with broad organizational impact,
    irreversible consequences, or multiple Technical Owners involved — the
    CE Leader convenes a Change Board. If the change affects one of your
    services, you are invited. Your role at the Change Board is governance:
    does the proposed change scope align with the service commitment? Does the
    impact assessment accurately characterize what your Service Customers will
    experience?

    You receive the change plan with at least 24 hours notice (except in urgent
    cases). You review it from a commitment and customer impact perspective.
    You raise concerns before the change executes, not after.

  Emergency Change notification and response
    For Emergency Changes — changes required immediately to prevent or recover
    from a service outage — the CE Leader may act with reduced approval processes.
    You are notified. In cases where the Emergency Change directly affects your
    service's committed availability, you may be consulted for a rapid governance
    confirmation. Post-execution: you receive confirmation that the Emergency
    Change occurred, along with its scope and impact.

  Catalog update verification after change completion
    When a change to your service is completed, CE updates the service catalog.
    You are notified. You may verify that the catalog entry accurately reflects
    the post-change committed state of your service.

9.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN PROBLEM MANAGEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Problem Management investigates structural service failures — recurring incidents,
root cause analysis, and the governance decision about what gets fixed, what gets
documented as a Known Error, and what gets accepted.

Your role in Problem Management is not investigative. It is governance.

  Receive escalation when a Problem investigation affects your service commitment
    When Problem Management is investigating a recurring failure pattern in one
    of your services, and the investigation reveals a structural issue that
    affects the SLA commitment you approved, the PM Leader escalates to you.

    The question they are bringing you is: given what we now know about the
    structural failure, is the current SLA commitment still appropriate? Does
    the organization need to accept a Known Error (workaround, no fix) while
    adjusting the customer's expectations? Or does the severity of the failure
    require a Change to maintain the commitment integrity?

    Your governance decision shapes the outcome of the Problem record — which
    in turn shapes what the SCM Worker communicates to the Service Customer
    about the service's reliability.

  Authorize change when structural fix is required
    When Problem Management determines that a structural fix is warranted and
    triggers a Change Request in Change Enablement, the change affects your
    service. You are involved in the governance chain: your approval of the
    change scope (particularly for COMPLEX changes) is the governance gate
    that ensures the fix aligns with what was committed.

  Be informed when Known Errors are created for your services
    When Problem Management creates a Known Error record for a failure in your
    service — meaning the root cause is documented and a workaround is provided,
    but no structural fix is planned at this time — you are informed. You need
    to know what the current known limitations of your service are. This
    information feeds into how the service commitment is communicated and
    governed going forward.

  Quarterly Problem Management review for your services
    Alongside the SLA review, your regular governance practice should include
    awareness of the open Known Error records for your services. These
    represent accepted structural limitations. If the accumulation of Known
    Errors for a service suggests that its committed quality can no longer be
    sustained, that is a governance signal that requires your decision —
    potentially a catalog modification, an SLA class adjustment, or a
    significant change investment.

10. YOUR RELATIONSHIP TO THE TECHNICAL OWNER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You and the Technical Owner are both in the Owner layer. You are peers in
governance accountability, serving complementary dimensions of the same service.

  You own the service commitment. They own the technical delivery.
    Your service commitment is what was promised to the Service Customer: the
    SLA class, the scope, the value. The Technical Owner is accountable for the
    Configuration Items — the technical elements that make the service delivery
    possible. Without technical delivery, your commitment is empty. Without a
    committed scope, their delivery has no governance anchor.

  You do not direct Technical Owners operationally.
    The Technical Owner receives work through Change Enablement and Problem
    Management. You do not assign Technical Owners to tasks. You do not contact
    them directly about incident resolution. When an incident affecting your
    service escalates to a Technical Owner, that escalation is governed through
    the Service Desk's escalation path — not through you.

  You are consulted when Technical Owner changes affect your service commitment.
    When a change in Technical Owner assignment for your service occurs — a
    new Technical Owner taking over a CI that composes your service — you are
    informed. The governance record must reflect the current accountability
    structure.

  In Problem Management investigations, you see the same problem from
  complementary angles.
    The Technical Owner provides the root cause analysis: what broke and why,
    technically. You provide the governance framing: what it means for the
    service commitment and what the organizational response should be. The PM
    Leader coordinates both perspectives to reach a decision.

11. YOUR RELATIONSHIP TO CSM AND THE SCM WORKER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The CSM function and your SCM Worker are the operational face of the commitment
you govern. They translate your service into a customer relationship. You govern
the structural quality of what they are committing to.

  The SCM Worker represents your service to Service Customers.
    Every communication the SCM Worker makes to a Service Customer about your
    service carries your commitment as the backing. When the SCM Worker says
    "this service is committed at SLA Class B," that commitment is yours. The
    SCM Worker is not making it up. They are communicating your governance
    position.

  You rely on the SCM Worker for customer relationship intelligence.
    The SCM Worker is your primary source of customer-side signal: what Service
    Customers are experiencing, what demands they are considering, what
    satisfaction or dissatisfaction they are expressing. This signal feeds your
    governance decisions about service evolution and SLA adequacy.

  You trust the CM Leader to bring escalations to you at the right level.
    The CM Leader resolves what can be resolved at the Leader layer. When
    something reaches you, it has already been through the CM Leader. The
    escalations you receive are real governance decisions — not operational
    questions that could have been answered without you.

  Your availability matters.
    A Service Owner who is consistently unreachable when the CM Leader escalates
    is a structural failure in the governance model. The escalation chain
    assumes that each level is responsive. When your level is unresponsive,
    the entire chain below you is left without the governance decision it needs.

12. THE ESCALATION CHAIN — WHEN IT REACHES YOU
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You are Level 3 in the CSM escalation chain:
  Level 1: SCM Worker
  Level 2: CM Leader
  Level 3: Service Owner — you
  Level 4: Governance Owner

When an escalation reaches you, it has already passed through the CM Leader.
The CM Leader's assessment is that this requires Service Owner authority.
That assessment is almost always correct.

What kinds of situations reach you:

  Commitment disputes
    A Service Customer disputes the scope, SLA class, or timeline of a
    commitment at the CM Leader level. The CM Leader cannot resolve it. You
    make the governance decision: adjust the commitment, hold the position, or
    escalate further.

  SLA breach escalation
    Every SLA breach for your services reaches you by design (SLM section
    above). High-severity breaches — Class A services, multiple concurrent
    breaches, contractual implications — require your explicit governance
    response.

  Service retirement disputes
    A Service Customer contests a retirement proposal. They believe the service
    is still needed or that insufficient replacement was provided. You make
    the governance decision: adjust the retirement plan, proceed as planned,
    or escalate.

  Structural quality questions
    Problem Management's findings suggest that a service's structural quality
    cannot sustain its current SLA commitment without significant intervention.
    The PM Leader and CM Leader jointly escalate to you. You decide: accept
    the structural limitation (Known Error), authorize a fix (Change), or
    adjust the commitment (catalog modification).

When an escalation reaches you, respond with governed decisions — not deferral.
The system is designed so that what reaches you genuinely requires your authority.
Using that authority promptly is not optional.

13. YOUR VIEW IN THE PORTAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your Experience Layer view is scoped to your service ownership. You see:

  Service portfolio status
    All services in your ownership, their current operational status, and any
    active incidents or SLA warnings affecting them.

  SLA performance overview
    Real-time and historical performance data for your services, across all
    Service Customers who have commitments on them. Breach events, warning
    events, and compliance trends.

  Active Problem records
    Any open Problem Management investigations for your services, including
    the current status of the investigation and the decision outcome when
    reached.

  Pending governance approvals
    Demand commitments awaiting your approval. Catalog change proposals
    requiring your authorization. Change Board items for complex changes
    affecting your services.

  Known Error register for your services
    Active Known Error records for your services — the current documented
    limitations and their associated workarounds.

As the platform matures, your view gains predictive capabilities: service
health trend analysis, forecast of likely demand patterns, and SLA risk
indicators based on operational data patterns.

Your portal view is an accountability dashboard — not a passive information
feed. What you see in it creates governance obligations. An SLA warning
visible on your dashboard is a governance signal that requires your attention.
A breach notification is an accountability event that requires your response.

14. WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A Service Owner is performing their role well when:

  Every service in your ownership has a current, accurate catalog entry.
    SLA class, scope, Technical Owner, Service Owner — all reflecting reality.
    Last reviewed within the last quarter.

  Every SLA commitment you approved was commitments that could be kept.
    When you approved the commitment at demand acceptance, you understood
    the operational profile of your service. You did not approve unrealistic
    targets or accept SLA classes that the service's technical delivery cannot
    sustain.

  Breach notifications produce governance responses, not documentation.
    When a breach occurs, you make a decision. Additional resources, emergency
    change, known limitation acceptance — one of these. Not silence.

  The PM Leader finds Known Error decisions straightforward to reach.
    Because your services have accurate catalog entries and SLA commitments
    aligned with their actual operational profile, the governance framing for
    Problem Management decisions is clear. You are not a bottleneck — you are
    a clear governance authority.

  The CM Leader escalates to you rarely.
    Not because issues do not exist — but because the SCM Workers and CM Leader
    have the clarity and authority to resolve most things at their level. When
    something does reach you, it is genuinely a Service Owner decision.

  Service retirements are clean.
    Services you retire are retired with customer acknowledgement, replacement
    guidance (where applicable), and a closed governance record. No service
    disappears from the catalog without a governed lifecycle closure.

  Your service commitments evolve with the services.
    As services grow more or less critical in your customers' operations, you
    initiate SLA class reviews and catalog updates. You do not wait for
    breach patterns to force the conversation.

The Owner layer is where governance is held. What you decide shapes the
commitments that SCM Workers make, the changes that CE executes, and the
quality that Service Users experience. That span of influence is your
accountability. It runs in both directions: upward to the Governance Owner
when something exceeds your authority, and downward through the entire
delivery chain when your governance decisions land.
