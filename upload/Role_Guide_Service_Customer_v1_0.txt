1.  WHO YOU ARE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You are a Service Customer.

In the CereBree uSMS model, a Service Customer is not an individual — it is
an organizational unit. A business division. A department. A company entity.
An external organization receiving managed IT services. What defines you as a
Service Customer is not your personal title — it is your formal relationship
with the IT organization.

That relationship includes:

  A service agreement
    What has been committed to you: which services, at which service level,
    for how many users, under which conditions. This is not aspirational. It
    is a governance commitment — tracked, monitored, and reported.

  Budget accountability
    You are accountable for the IT spend your organizational unit consumes.
    Services are not free. Demands have cost implications. SLA classes carry
    resource implications. Your role includes governing how IT resources are
    consumed by your organizational unit.

  User governance
    You define which services your users may access. No user in your unit
    can access a service you have not authorized. This is not an
    administrative technicality — it is your governance authority over
    the services your team uses.

  Escalation authority
    When commitments are not met, you have the right — and the structural
    backing — to hold the IT organization accountable. The SLA governance
    model makes this concrete and governed, not political.

In practice, one or more people in your organization act in the Service
Customer role: a department lead, an IT liaison, a divisional manager. But
regardless of who speaks on behalf of the Service Customer, the role is always
at the organizational level, not the individual level.

This distinction matters. It means your agreement with IT is formal, your
commitments are tracked, and your services are governed — not managed by
whoever happens to answer the phone this week.

2.  YOUR NAMED CONTACT: THE SCM WORKER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

You have one named contact in the IT organization: your SCM Worker.

SCM stands for Service Customer Manager. Your SCM Worker is not a helpdesk
agent. They are not a ticket processor. They are your dedicated governance
contact — the person who translates your business needs into governed IT
decisions and who holds the IT organization accountable to your commitments.

Your SCM Worker is your single thread of communication with the IT
organization. When something changes, they tell you. When you need something,
you tell them. When something goes wrong, they carry the governance response
back to you. You do not deal with engineers, change teams, or technical owners
directly — that is their job.

What your SCM Worker does for you:

  Receives and assesses your demands
    When you need a new service, a modification to an existing one, or want
    to retire a service your team no longer uses, you bring it to your SCM
    Worker. They assess the demand against the service catalog, determine
    whether it is already covered by an existing offering, and either redirect
    you to a solution that already exists — or formally process your demand
    through the IT organization.

  Manages your service commitments
    Your SCM Worker knows exactly which services have been committed to your
    organizational unit, at which SLA class, and what the current compliance
    status is. They work from governed records, not memory or informal notes.

  Reports on SLA performance
    You receive regular SLA performance reports. These are not marketing
    summaries — they are governance reports: what was committed, what was
    delivered, where there were breaches, and what is being done about them.
    When there is a warning before a breach occurs, your SCM Worker alerts
    you proactively. You are never the last to know.

  Maintains your catalog view
    Your SCM Worker ensures that the service catalog — as it applies to your
    organizational unit — reflects your current agreements at all times. When
    a service changes, they manage the process. When a service is retired,
    they communicate it. Nothing appears in your catalog without governance
    backing.

  Is your contact throughout the entire demand lifecycle
    Once you submit a demand, your SCM Worker stays with it until it is
    fulfilled. Even when execution is handed to Change Enablement — the team
    that builds and delivers the change — your SCM Worker retains
    communication ownership. You receive progress, delays, and completion
    confirmation through your SCM Worker. Never through engineers. Never
    through a system message without context.

This is not a convenience. It is a structural design decision. Every
interaction between your organizational unit and the IT organization goes
through a governed channel — one that creates records, applies process rules,
and produces traceable outcomes.

You do not have multiple contacts in IT. You have one: your SCM Worker.

3.  WHAT YOU CAN DO
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

3.1  Submit a Demand

A Demand is a formal request for a change to the service landscape: a new
service for your team, a modification to an existing service (scope, access,
capacity, SLA class), or the retirement of a service you no longer need.

Demands are submitted to your SCM Worker — not to a portal form alone, not to
the Service Desk, and not to Technical Owners directly.

The Demand lifecycle:

  NEW
    Your demand has been received and recorded. A Demand Record exists.

  UNDER_REVIEW
    Your SCM Worker is assessing the demand. They check whether it is already
    covered by the service catalog. They evaluate technical feasibility and
    portfolio alignment. They may ask you clarifying questions during this phase.

  QUOTED
    The IT organization has assessed your demand as feasible and not already
    covered. Your SCM Worker presents you with a formal quote: scope, timeline,
    SLA class, and resource or cost implications. You review it and decide.

  ACCEPTED
    You have approved the quote. Your demand transitions to Change Enablement
    for technical execution. Your SCM Worker stays with it and keeps you
    informed throughout.

  IN_CHANGE
    The technical work is underway. The change team is building, modifying, or
    retiring the service as scoped. Your SCM Worker provides you with progress
    updates at defined milestones.

  FULFILLED
    The service has been created, modified, or retired. The service catalog has
    been updated to reflect the new reality. Your commitment is live.

  CLOSED
    The Demand Record is formally closed. The governance record is complete.

If a demand is assessed as already covered by an existing offering, it is
REDIRECTED — your SCM Worker informs you and points you to the relevant
service or request type. If a demand is assessed as infeasible or outside the
agreed scope, it is REJECTED — with a documented reason, communicated to you
by your SCM Worker.

A rejected or redirected demand is not a failure. It is the system working:
you get an honest assessment, not a promise that cannot be kept.


3.2  Approve or Reject a Quote

When your SCM Worker issues a quote, you have the authority to accept or reject
it. This is a formal governance decision.

What a quote contains:

  – The scope of the demand as understood and assessed by the IT organization
  – Estimated effort and timeline
  – The SLA class that will govern the resulting service or modification
  – Any cost or resource implications
  – Any dependencies or constraints that affect delivery

If the quote matches your expectations, you accept it. Formal acceptance
commits both parties: the IT organization to delivering what was quoted, you to
the associated commitments. From this point forward, SLA monitoring applies.

If the quote does not match your expectations — the scope is wrong, the
timeline is unacceptable, the SLA class is not what you need — you reject it.
Rejection returns the demand to your SCM Worker for reassessment or formal
closure, with your documented reason. You are never required to accept a quote
that does not serve your organizational needs.

Your CM Leader (the leader of the CSM function) approves every quote before it
reaches you. This means you are receiving a governance-validated commitment —
not an estimate produced under pressure.


3.3  Track Demand Status

From the moment a demand is submitted, its status is visible to you in the
portal. You do not need to ask your SCM Worker where your demand is — the
system tells you.

Your SCM Worker supplements this with direct communication at key transitions:
when a quote is ready, when the demand enters change execution, when milestones
are reached, and when fulfillment is confirmed.

You are never in the dark about your demand's progress.


3.4  Hold IT Accountable for SLA Commitments

This is one of your most important rights as a Service Customer — and it is
structurally enforced, not merely promised.

Every service committed to your organizational unit carries an SLA class. That
class defines the response and resolution targets that apply when things go
wrong, and the fulfillment targets that apply when requests are made.

The SLA monitoring system watches these targets continuously. When a target is
approaching breach — at 80% of the clock consumed — you are notified via your
SCM Worker, proactively, before the breach occurs. When a breach happens at
100%, you are notified immediately, with context: what happened, which service,
what is being done.

You receive a regular SLA performance report for your service portfolio:
  – SLA compliance per service, per period
  – Any breaches that occurred, with root cause context where available
  – The status of any ongoing remediation

If you identify a pattern of SLA underperformance, you have the right to
escalate. Your SCM Worker escalates to the CM Leader. The CM Leader escalates
to the Service Owner. This chain is defined, named, and fast.

The system is built to make poor performance visible — to you and to the IT
organization simultaneously. SLA governance is not a courtesy. It is a
structural commitment.


3.5  Govern What Your Users Can Access

Your organizational unit is the governance authority for the services your
users consume. No user in your unit can access a service or request type that
your Service Customer agreement does not cover.

This entitlement model works as follows:

  Your agreement with the IT organization defines which services are active for
  your unit and at which level. These are your entitlements.

  Your users inherit those entitlements by virtue of belonging to your
  organizational unit. They can request and receive what your agreement covers.
  They cannot request or receive what it does not cover — and the system
  enforces this automatically.

  More specific assignments override broader ones. If a particular user within
  your unit needs access that differs from the unit default, that can be
  assigned individually — still within the governed model, still traceable.

When you submit a demand for a new service or a scope change, you are
exercising your governance authority over this entitlement structure. When a
user tells you they cannot access something, the answer is either in the
existing entitlement model (they are not entitled) or in a demand to expand
the scope — which you initiate as Service Customer.

4.  WHAT YOU CANNOT DO — AND WHY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The following are structural boundaries — not policies that can be waived.

  You cannot access the Service Desk.
    The Service Desk serves Service Users — individuals using services in their
    daily work. Its four processes (Incident Management, Request Fulfillment,
    Access Management, and Event Management) are for operational service
    interactions, not for organizational governance. If something in your unit
    is not working, the affected user reports it via the Service Desk. If you
    need a new or modified service, that comes through Customer Service
    Management and your SCM Worker.

  You cannot contact Technical Owners directly.
    Technical Owners are reachable only through defined governance paths:
    Change Enablement and Problem Management. Direct contact bypasses the
    governance model, creates undocumented commitments, and consumes the
    Technical Owner's attention outside any governed structure. If you need
    something from a Technical Owner's domain, it flows through your SCM
    Worker and the appropriate process.

  You cannot report incidents.
    Incidents are reported by Service Users through the Service Desk. If a
    service your unit depends on is disrupted, your users report it through
    their Service Desk access. Your SCM Worker is informed through SLA
    monitoring when those incidents approach or breach their targets.

  You cannot approve service changes.
    Change approval authority belongs to the Change Enablement function. You
    accept or reject quotes — but the execution and technical approval of
    changes is governed by the CE Leader, not by you. The same person cannot
    both request a change and approve its execution. This separation exists
    by design to protect the governance integrity of the system.

  You cannot view other customers' service data.
    Tenant isolation is structural. Your service portfolio, SLA performance
    data, demand history, and user entitlements are visible only to you and
    to the IT organization acting on your behalf. No other Service Customer
    can see your data. This applies equally in both internal and B2B contexts.

  You cannot modify the service catalog directly.
    The catalog is modified through Change Enablement only. CSM triggers and
    oversees catalog changes. Change Enablement executes them. This separation
    exists because a catalog that can be edited informally is a catalog that
    cannot be trusted.

These boundaries are not bureaucratic obstacles. They are the structural design
that makes your service relationship governable, your commitments enforceable,
and your data protected. Every boundary that applies to you also protects you.

5.  YOUR SERVICE PORTFOLIO
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your service portfolio is the set of services that have been committed to your
organizational unit and are currently active. It is maintained in the uSeC
Service Catalog and governed through Service Catalog Management.

The catalog describes each service through nine structured perspectives: what
it does, who it serves, what SLA level has been committed, what support levels
apply, what can be requested from it, who owns its quality, who delivers it
technically, whether it is a commodity or a custom offering, and which service
package it belongs to.

This means you always know what you have agreed to. Nothing in your portfolio
exists without a governed description. The catalog is not a marketing brochure.
It is your governance record of what IT has committed to deliver.

Service Packages
Your services may be organized into service packages — logical bundles of
related services designed for coherent consumption. A standard employee
onboarding package might include identity provisioning, device setup, email
configuration, and network access as a single governed bundle. Each service
within the package retains its own lifecycle, owner, and SLA — but the package
simplifies how you govern the overall relationship.

When a service in your portfolio changes — because you submitted a demand,
because a technical change was required, or because the catalog was updated —
your SCM Worker communicates this to you before and after the change. The
catalog is always current. You are always informed.

6.  SLA CLASSES: WHAT WAS COMMITTED TO YOU
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Every service in your portfolio is assigned an SLA class. There are four:

  Class A — Mission Critical
    For services where any outage has immediate, significant business impact
    and no acceptable alternative exists while the service is down. Highest
    availability targets. Fastest incident response and resolution commitments.

  Class B — Business Important
    For services important to normal operations but where brief disruption can
    be managed with workarounds. Strong reliability. Defined response and
    resolution windows. Core supporting services.

  Class C — Standard Operational
    For services with predictable, manageable impact when disrupted. Standard
    response and resolution windows. Services that support work without stopping
    it when disrupted.

  Class D — Advisory / Best Effort
    For consulting, exploratory, or low-criticality services. No committed
    clock-based response or resolution targets at the Minimal Stage. Best-effort
    delivery model.

The SLA class for each service in your portfolio was established when your
demand was accepted. It is documented in the catalog and enforced through the
SLA monitoring system.

If the committed class no longer matches the actual criticality of the service
in your operations — for example, a service has grown more business-critical
than originally anticipated — that is a conversation to have with your SCM
Worker. It may lead to a catalog modification demand, processed through the
normal governance path.

SLA classes are not marketing tiers. They carry concrete commitments that the
IT organization is monitored against continuously. A breach triggers immediate
governance response — not a post-hoc explanation.

7.  YOUR VIEW IN THE PORTAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The CereBree Experience Layer is your governed entry point into the system.
One URL. One login. One view of everything relevant to your role and scope.

What you see is governed by your entitlements. You see exactly what is relevant
to your Service Customer scope — nothing more, nothing less. If data is not in
your scope, it is not present in your view. This is not a permission filter
applied on top of a shared dataset. It is entitlement-based rendering.

What your portal view contains:

  Your service portfolio
    All services committed to your organizational unit, their current status,
    SLA class, and relevant catalog information.

  SLA performance dashboard
    Real-time and historical performance data for your services. Which services
    are within target. Which carry active warnings. Which have experienced
    breaches and what the current remediation status is.

  Demand pipeline
    All active and recent demands in their current lifecycle state. What is
    under review. What has been quoted and is awaiting your decision. What is
    in change execution. What has been fulfilled.

  Notifications and alerts
    SLA warnings, breach notifications, demand status changes, and catalog
    updates. Every notification is backed by a governance event. Nothing appears
    without cause.

  Your recent activity
    A cross-domain aggregation of your service-related activity: demands
    submitted, quotes reviewed, services activated or retired.

As the platform matures through the stage model, your portal view gains
additional capabilities: analytics, usage and cost transparency, demand
forecasting, and deeper SLA performance insight. At every stage, the
principle is the same: you see what is relevant, in real time, without
having to ask.

8.  COGNI: YOUR AI ASSISTANT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Cogni is the CereBree AI Assistant. It is available to you within the portal,
within your governed scope and entitlement boundaries. Cogni does not operate
outside those boundaries. It does not access other customers' data. It does
not make commitments on behalf of the IT organization.

What Cogni does for you as a Service Customer:

  Smart Stage
    – SLA performance at a glance: "Your top 3 services by SLA compliance
      this month: Email (99.8%), ERP (98.2%), Data Warehouse (94.1% —
      one breach recorded this period)."
    – Demand status: "Your demand for the collaboration platform is currently
      in Change Enablement. Status: Planning. Expected completion: 12 April."
    – Breach communication drafting: when an SLA breach occurs, Cogni prepares
      the initial communication for your SCM Worker to review and send — with
      root cause context from Problem Management included where available.

  Mature Stage
    – Demand forecasting: based on usage patterns and demand history, Cogni
      identifies capacity or service gaps before they become demands. Your SCM
      Worker is notified to discuss proactively.
    – Usage and cost transparency: a breakdown of service consumption against
      your committed baseline, by service.
    – Commitment evolution guidance: when SLA performance patterns suggest a
      service is under-committed (e.g., Class B with consistent Class A
      thresholds), Cogni surfaces this for your SCM Worker to discuss.

What Cogni never does:

  – Surface another customer's service data or SLA performance
  – Make or modify service commitments without SCM Worker and CM Leader approval
  – Communicate SLA breaches to you without SCM Worker review
  – Replace a governance decision with an automated output

Cogni is an intelligence layer, not a decision layer. Accountability stays with
your SCM Worker, the CM Leader, and the Service Owner. Cogni makes them faster
and better informed — it does not replace their judgment.

9.  ESCALATION PATHS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

When something requires escalation beyond your normal SCM Worker interaction,
the escalation path is defined, named, and fast.

  Level 1 — SCM Worker
    First contact for all governance topics: demand questions, SLA concerns,
    catalog questions, status updates, quote negotiations.

  Level 2 — CM Leader (Customer Management Leader)
    If your SCM Worker cannot resolve an issue, or if you need to escalate
    a dispute about a quote, rejection, or SLA commitment, the CM Leader is
    the next authority. The CM Leader holds operational accountability for the
    entire CSM function.

  Level 3 — Service Owner
    If a dispute involves the quality, scope, or SLA performance of a specific
    service, the Service Owner is the accountability holder for that service.
    The CM Leader escalates to the Service Owner when required.

  Level 4 — Governance Owner
    In cases where service governance decisions affect organizational or
    contractual commitments at the highest level, the Governance Owner holds
    ultimate authority. This level involves decisions affecting the structural
    integrity of the service relationship.

You do not navigate these escalation levels yourself. Your SCM Worker manages
the escalation process on your behalf. Your role is to communicate clearly
what the issue is and what resolution you need.

In a B2B context — where you are an external organization receiving managed
IT services — these escalation paths carry contractual weight. A breach that
is an internal governance matter at Level 2 may be a contractual accountability
matter at Level 3 or 4.

10. YOUR RELATIONSHIP TO THE BACK-OFFICE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Behind your SCM Worker and the visible processes of Customer Service
Management, the uSMS Back-Office operates continuously. You do not interact
with it directly. That is the point.

  Problem Management
    When a service in your portfolio experiences recurring failures, Problem
    Management investigates the structural root cause. Its job is not to close
    tickets — it is to make a proportionate governance decision: fix the
    structural issue permanently (triggering a change), document it as a known
    limitation with a workaround, or accept it. When Problem Management's
    findings are relevant to your SLA commitments, your SCM Worker communicates
    them to you with the context you need to understand what is being done
    and why.

  Change Enablement
    All structural changes to your services — new services, modifications,
    retirements — are executed by Change Enablement. CE is the only function
    authorized to write to the service catalog. When your demand transitions
    to IN_CHANGE, Change Enablement is the execution authority. You do not
    interact with CE directly. Your SCM Worker does.

  Service Configuration Management
    Technical Owners maintain the current state of the configuration items
    that compose your services. This data makes incident investigations,
    change assessments, and root cause analyses accurate. When your SCM
    Worker reports on a service change, the configuration data is the
    technical foundation of that report.

  Knowledge Management
    Every incident resolution, every root cause analysis, every known
    workaround is governed knowledge within the system. The Known Error
    Database means recurring issues are surfaced immediately when they recur
    — rather than being rediscovered each time. This benefits your users
    directly: faster incident resolution, more consistent service behavior.

The Back-Office is invisible to you — and that invisibility is a feature,
not a gap. It means the IT organization handles structural complexity so that
your interaction remains clean, governed, and focused on what matters to you.

11. WHAT HAPPENS WHEN THINGS GO WRONG
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Services fail. Commitments are sometimes missed. The uSMS system does not
promise perfection — it promises governance.

When a service your unit depends on is disrupted:
  Your users report the disruption through the Service Desk. The Service Desk
  categorizes the incident, checks for known errors, applies workarounds where
  available, and works to restore service. SLA monitoring tracks the incident
  against the committed targets for your service's SLA class.

  If the incident approaches breach (80% of the SLA clock consumed), your SCM
  Worker is notified and alerts you proactively.

  If a breach occurs, you are notified immediately with full context: what
  happened, which service, what is being done. The Service Owner for the
  affected service is also notified. Governance action is triggered — not just
  explained after the fact.

When a service repeatedly fails in the same way:
  If the same service generates three or more incidents within seven days,
  Problem Management is automatically triggered to investigate the structural
  root cause. You do not need to escalate. The system detects the pattern and
  governs it.

  Problem Management produces a decision: fix the structural issue (triggering
  a change), document it as a known limitation (with a workaround accessible
  to the Service Desk), or accept it as proportionate. You receive the outcome
  from your SCM Worker with context — including what the decision means for
  your SLA commitments going forward.

When a committed service is not delivered on time:
  Demand fulfillment adherence is part of your SCM Worker's accountability.
  If a demand's committed timeline is not met, your SCM Worker provides you
  with an updated timeline and a documented reason. This is governed
  communication — not an informal apology.

When SLA performance is persistently below what was committed:
  Your SCM Worker and CM Leader have the governance tools to respond. Problem
  Management investigation, change authorization, escalation to the Service
  Owner — all are available. Your role is to ensure the governance system
  knows what you are experiencing. The structural response is the IT
  organization's responsibility.

The governance model does not make failures disappear. It makes them visible,
attributed, and actioned. That is the difference between a service relationship
and a service conversation.
