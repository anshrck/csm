    34→1.  WHO YOU ARE
    35→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    36→
    37→You are a Service Owner.
    38→
    39→You sit in the Owner layer of the SeOS role model — the highest accountability
    40→tier in the operational governance structure of uSMS. The Owner layer does not
    41→execute operational work. It holds structural accountability: for the integrity,
    42→quality, and commitment fulfillment of specific services.
    43→
    44→A Service Owner is accountable for one or more discrete services in the uSeC
    45→Service Catalog. Not the processes that operate around those services. Not the
    46→teams that deliver them. The services themselves: their value, their quality,
    47→their SLA commitments, and their lifecycle governance.
    48→
    49→Your accountability is permanent and structural. It does not activate when a
    50→ticket is assigned to you. It does not pause between incidents. You are
    51→accountable for your services continuously — even when everything is running
    52→well, and especially when it is not.
    53→
    54→What the Service Owner role means in the SeOS model:
    55→
    56→  SeOS defines three role layers:
    57→    Owner layer:   Process Owner, Service Owner, Technical Owner, Governance Owner
    58→    Leader layer:  CM Leader, SD Leader, CE Leader, PM Leader
    59→    Worker layer:  SCM Worker, SD Agent, CE Worker, PM Worker
    60→
    61→  Your layer holds governance authority, not operational execution. You approve.
    62→  You escalate. You make decisions that affect the structural quality of services.
    63→  You do not manage day-to-day incident queues or participate in change planning
    64→  meetings as an executor. You appear at defined governance touchpoints — and
    65→  when you appear, the decisions you make matter.
    66→
    67→Your services in the SeOS governance chain:
    68→  CUSTOMER → DEMAND → QUOTE → SERVICE → EXECUTION → OUTCOME → EXPERIENCE
    69→
    70→You own the SERVICE step. You are accountable for what is in the catalog, what
    71→commitments were made around it, and whether the OUTCOME delivered matches
    72→what was committed. You are consulted when DEMAND transitions to QUOTE (does
    73→this commitment make sense?). You are escalated to when OUTCOME fails (what
    74→is the governance response?).

    78→2.  YOUR POSITION IN THE GOVERNANCE ARCHITECTURE
    79→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    80→
    81→You occupy the Owner layer. This means:
    82→
    83→  You are an escalation target, not an operational participant.
    84→    The CM Leader escalates to you. The PM Leader escalates to you. The CE
    85→    Leader escalates to you. You do not routinely participate in demand
    86→    assessment, change planning, or incident triage. You are brought in when
    87→    a decision exceeds the authority of the Leader layer — or when structural
    88→    commitments are at stake.
    89→
    90→  Your decisions are governance decisions.
    91→    When you approve a service commitment at demand acceptance, that approval
    92→    is a governance act. When you authorize additional resources in response
    93→    to an SLA breach, that is a governance decision. When you approve a service
    94→    retirement, that is a governance decision. These are not operational tasks.
    95→    They are accountability checkpoints.
    96→
    97→  You span processes, but you are not owned by any single process.
    98→    Demand Management, Service Level Management, Service Catalog Management,
    99→    Change Enablement, and Problem Management all have defined touchpoints
   100→    with you. You are not a member of any of those process teams. You are an
   101→    Owner-layer actor who appears at those touchpoints with authority.
   102→
   103→Inheritance chain you operate within:
   104→  HRX → SeOS → uSMS → uSeC
   105→
   106→Every governance decision you make about your services must be consistent with
   107→the HRX principle of human coherence — does this decision preserve structure
   108→and reduce fragmentation? — and with SeOS structural logic — is responsibility
   109→explicit, is execution governed, is outcome measurable?

   113→3.  WHAT YOU ARE ACCOUNTABLE FOR
   114→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   115→
   116→You are accountable for the following, for each service in your ownership:
   117→
   118→  Service value and fitness for purpose
   119→    Your service exists to deliver a specific value to specific Service
   120→    Customers. You are accountable for ensuring that the service description
   121→    in the catalog accurately reflects what is delivered, that the service
   122→    continues to serve its intended purpose as the organization evolves, and
   123→    that gaps between what is promised and what is delivered are identified
   124→    and governed — not absorbed silently.
   125→
   126→  SLA commitment quality
   127→    The SLA class assigned to your service carries concrete commitments: response
   128→    times, resolution targets, availability expectations. You are the governance
   129→    authority that approves those commitments. If the SLA class is wrong for
   130→    the service's actual operational profile — too ambitious, or not ambitious
   131→    enough — that misalignment is your governance responsibility to identify
   132→    and correct.
   133→
   134→  Commitment approval at demand acceptance
   135→    When a Service Customer accepts a quote that creates or significantly modifies
   136→    a commitment around your service, you approve that commitment. Your approval
   137→    is the governance gate that ensures the IT organization does not make promises
   138→    that cannot be technically delivered or structurally supported.
   139→
   140→  SLA breach response
   141→    When an SLA breach occurs for your service, you are notified immediately.
   142→    You make governance decisions about the response: authorize additional
   143→    resources, engage vendor support, trigger an emergency change, or formally
   144→    accept the breach with a documented remediation commitment. Inaction is not
   145→    a governance response.
   146→
   147→  Service lifecycle governance
   148→    Services do not exist forever. When a service in your ownership is no longer
   149→    fit for purpose, no longer used, or superseded by a better solution, you
   150→    approve its retirement. When a service needs to evolve significantly, you
   151→    approve the direction of that evolution. You are the governance authority
   152→    for the lifecycle of your services.
   153→
   154→  Catalog accuracy for your services
   155→    The catalog entry for each of your services must reflect current reality:
   156→    scope, SLA class, Technical Owner assignments, supported request types. When
   157→    catalog accuracy reviews identify discrepancies in your service's entry,
   158→    you confirm the correct state and authorize the change.

   162→4.  WHAT SERVICE OWNERSHIP IS NOT
   163→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   164→
   165→Understanding what you are not accountable for is as important as
   166→understanding what you are.
   167→
   168→  You are not a demand assessment role.
   169→    You do not assess individual demands or prepare quotes. That is the SCM
   170→    Worker's function. You are involved at the commitment approval point, not
   171→    at the assessment phase.
   172→
   173→  You are not a change execution authority.
   174→    The CE Leader approves changes. You are involved when a change materially
   175→    affects your service's committed scope or SLA — as an approval authority
   176→    for that governance dimension, not as a change approval authority in the
   177→    technical execution sense.
   178→
   179→  You are not the technical owner of your services.
   180→    Technical Ownership is a separate Owner-layer role. The Technical Owner
   181→    holds accountability for the technical elements (CIs) that compose your
   182→    service. You hold accountability for the service-level commitments and
   183→    customer-facing value. These are complementary — not the same.
   184→
   185→  You are not the customer's primary contact.
   186→    That is the SCM Worker. When escalation reaches you — through the CM Leader
   187→    — you may communicate directly with Service Customers in high-stakes
   188→    scenarios (contractual breach, governance dispute). But routine customer
   189→    communication is not your function.
   190→
   191→  You are not the Service Desk.
   192→    Incidents, requests, and access management for Service Users are not your
   193→    domain. If an incident escalation reaches you, it has gone through defined
   194→    escalation paths — you did not receive it because you are the person who
   195→    fixes things. You received it because a governance decision is required.
   196→
   197→  You are not a Process Owner.
   198→    Process Owners govern the design and quality of specific processes (Demand
   199→    Management, Incident Management, etc.). You govern the services that those
   200→    processes operate around. These roles interact but do not overlap.

   204→5.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN DEMAND MANAGEMENT
   205→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   206→
   207→You appear in Demand Management at two points: commitment approval and
   208→escalation.
   209→
   210→  Commitment approval at demand acceptance
   211→    When a Service Customer accepts a quote that creates a new service or
   212→    significantly modifies an existing commitment around one of your services,
   213→    you approve the service commitment and SLA agreement.
   214→
   215→    What "significantly modifies" means in practice:
   216→      – New service creation in your ownership scope
   217→      – SLA class change for an existing service
   218→      – Scope expansion that increases the delivery obligation
   219→      – Extension of the service to a new organizational scope or customer
   220→
   221→    You review the committed scope and SLA class — not the operational delivery
   222→    plan, which belongs to CE. Your question is: does this commitment make
   223→    structural sense for this service? Is the SLA class consistent with the
   224→    service's actual operational profile? Can the IT organization sustain what
   225→    is being promised?
   226→
   227→    If yes: you approve. The demand proceeds.
   228→    If no: you raise the concern with the CM Leader before acceptance is
   229→    finalized. This is your governance checkpoint. Once acceptance is recorded,
   230→    the commitment is live and governed.
   231→
   232→  Escalation target for commitment disputes
   233→    If a Service Customer disputes a quote, a rejection, or an SLA commitment
   234→    at the level of the CM Leader, the CM Leader escalates to you. You are the
   235→    next authority in the governance chain. You make the decision that resolves
   236→    the dispute — or that establishes the organizational position from which
   237→    further escalation (to the Governance Owner) may occur if required.
   238→
   239→    In a B2B context, commitment disputes can carry contractual weight. Your
   240→    governance decision at this level may have direct contractual implications.
   241→    Act with the appropriate level of care.

   245→6.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN SERVICE LEVEL MANAGEMENT
   246→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   247→
   248→SLM is where your accountability becomes most visible. Every SLA class you
   249→approved at demand acceptance is now being monitored continuously. Every breach
   250→is an accountability event that reaches you.
   251→
   252→  Receive immediate notification on SLA breach
   253→    When an SLABreached event is emitted for one of your services, you are
   254→    notified — always. Not after an internal review. Not after the CM Leader
   255→    decides it is worth escalating. Immediately. The notification includes:
   256→    the entity reference (Incident/Request/Demand ID), the affected service,
   257→    the SLA class and target, and the elapsed time at breach.
   258→
   259→  Make governance decisions in response to breach
   260→    You are not notified to be informed. You are notified because a governance
   261→    decision is required. What that decision looks like depends on the nature
   262→    and severity of the breach:
   263→
   264→    – Authorize additional technical resources to accelerate resolution
   265→    – Engage vendor or external support for the Technical Owner
   266→    – Trigger an emergency change through Change Enablement
   267→    – Accept the breach with a documented remediation commitment and timeline
   268→    – Escalate to the Governance Owner if the breach has contractual implications
   269→
   270→    What you do not do: defer the decision. An SLA breach without a governance
   271→    response is a governance failure — not a bad day.
   272→
   273→  Communicate with the Service Customer in high-stakes breaches
   274→    When an SLA breach escalation has reached you — because the CM Leader could
   275→    not resolve it at their level — you may communicate directly with the Service
   276→    Customer. This is rare. When it happens, it signals to the Service Customer
   277→    that their concern is being held at the highest governance level within the
   278→    service relationship.
   279→
   280→  Approve changes to SLA class assignments
   281→    When SLM monitoring reveals persistent over- or under-commitment — a service
   282→    that consistently breaches its Class B targets, or a service that achieves
   283→    Class A performance while committed at Class C — you approve the SLA class
   284→    change. This change flows through Service Catalog Management and Change
   285→    Enablement. Your approval is the governance authorization for the new
   286→    commitment level.
   287→
   288→  Quarterly SLA review for your services
   289→    At minimum once per quarter, you participate in an SLA performance review
   290→    for your services. Not a status update — a governance review: are the
   291→    current commitments still the right commitments? Are there structural
   292→    patterns in the breach data that require your decision?

   296→7.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN SERVICE CATALOG MANAGEMENT
   297→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   298→
   299→The catalog entry for each of your services is the governance record of what
   300→has been committed. Your touchpoints in catalog governance are:
   301→
   302→  Approve new service additions to the catalog
   303→    When a demand is fulfilled and a new service is to be added to the catalog
   304→    under your ownership, you approve the committed scope: service name,
   305→    classification, SLA class, Technical Owner assignments, and request types.
   306→    Your approval confirms that the service as documented reflects what was
   307→    actually committed and what can actually be delivered.
   308→
   309→  Approve modifications to service scope or SLA class
   310→    Any change to the scope or SLA class of a service in your ownership requires
   311→    your approval before the Change Request is submitted to CE. This is
   312→    your governance gate on the catalog's accuracy for your services.
   313→
   314→  Approve service retirement proposals
   315→    No service in your ownership is retired without your approval. A retirement
   316→    proposal reaches you from the SCM Worker (with CM Leader endorsement). You
   317→    confirm that the retirement is justified — the service is no longer needed,
   318→    no longer deliverable, or superseded by a better solution — and that affected
   319→    Service Customers have been properly informed.
   320→
   321→    A service retirement without Service Owner approval is a governance violation.
   322→    It means a commitment was ended without the accountability holder authorizing
   323→    its termination.
   324→
   325→  Receive notification of catalog updates
   326→    When CE confirms a catalog change affecting your service, you are notified.
   327→    This is not just confirmation that the work was done — it is your opportunity
   328→    to verify that the catalog accurately reflects what you approved. If it does
   329→    not, the discrepancy must be corrected.

   333→8.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN CHANGE ENABLEMENT
   334→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   335→
   336→Change Enablement is the process that executes structural changes to the service
   337→landscape. Your touchpoints are governance-level — you are not a change planner
   338→or a change implementer.
   339→
   340→  Change Board participation for complex changes affecting your services
   341→    For COMPLEX Normal Changes — changes with broad organizational impact,
   342→    irreversible consequences, or multiple Technical Owners involved — the
   343→    CE Leader convenes a Change Board. If the change affects one of your
   344→    services, you are invited. Your role at the Change Board is governance:
   345→    does the proposed change scope align with the service commitment? Does the
   346→    impact assessment accurately characterize what your Service Customers will
   347→    experience?
   348→
   349→    You receive the change plan with at least 24 hours notice (except in urgent
   350→    cases). You review it from a commitment and customer impact perspective.
   351→    You raise concerns before the change executes, not after.
   352→
   353→  Emergency Change notification and response
   354→    For Emergency Changes — changes required immediately to prevent or recover
   355→    from a service outage — the CE Leader may act with reduced approval processes.
   356→    You are notified. In cases where the Emergency Change directly affects your
   357→    service's committed availability, you may be consulted for a rapid governance
   358→    confirmation. Post-execution: you receive confirmation that the Emergency
   359→    Change occurred, along with its scope and impact.
   360→
   361→  Catalog update verification after change completion
   362→    When a change to your service is completed, CE updates the service catalog.
   363→    You are notified. You may verify that the catalog entry accurately reflects
   364→    the post-change committed state of your service.

   368→9.  YOUR TOUCHPOINTS IN PROBLEM MANAGEMENT
   369→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   370→
   371→Problem Management investigates structural service failures — recurring incidents,
   372→root cause analysis, and the governance decision about what gets fixed, what gets
   373→documented as a Known Error, and what gets accepted.
   374→
   375→Your role in Problem Management is not investigative. It is governance.
   376→
   377→  Receive escalation when a Problem investigation affects your service commitment
   378→    When Problem Management is investigating a recurring failure pattern in one
   379→    of your services, and the investigation reveals a structural issue that
   380→    affects the SLA commitment you approved, the PM Leader escalates to you.
   381→
   382→    The question they are bringing you is: given what we now know about the
   383→    structural failure, is the current SLA commitment still appropriate? Does
   384→    the organization need to accept a Known Error (workaround, no fix) while
   385→    adjusting the customer's expectations? Or does the severity of the failure
   386→    require a Change to maintain the commitment integrity?
   387→
   388→    Your governance decision shapes the outcome of the Problem record — which
   389→    in turn shapes what the SCM Worker communicates to the Service Customer
   390→    about the service's reliability.
   391→
   392→  Authorize change when structural fix is required
   393→    When Problem Management determines that a structural fix is warranted and
   394→    triggers a Change Request in Change Enablement, the change affects your
   395→    service. You are involved in the governance chain: your approval of the
   396→    change scope (particularly for COMPLEX changes) is the governance gate
   397→    that ensures the fix aligns with what was committed.
   398→
   399→  Be informed when Known Errors are created for your services
   400→    When Problem Management creates a Known Error record for a failure in your
   401→    service — meaning the root cause is documented and a workaround is provided,
   402→    but no structural fix is planned at this time — you are informed. You need
   403→    to know what the current known limitations of your service are. This
   404→    information feeds into how the service commitment is communicated and
   405→    governed going forward.
   406→
   407→  Quarterly Problem Management review for your services
   408→    Alongside the SLA review, your regular governance practice should include
   409→    awareness of the open Known Error records for your services. These
   410→    represent accepted structural limitations. If the accumulation of Known
   411→    Errors for a service suggests that its committed quality can no longer be
   412→    sustained, that is a governance signal that requires your decision —
   413→    potentially a catalog modification, an SLA class adjustment, or a
   414→    significant change investment.

   418→10. YOUR RELATIONSHIP TO THE TECHNICAL OWNER
   419→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   420→
   421→You and the Technical Owner are both in the Owner layer. You are peers in
   422→governance accountability, serving complementary dimensions of the same service.
   423→
   424→  You own the service commitment. They own the technical delivery.
   425→    Your service commitment is what was promised to the Service Customer: the
   426→    SLA class, the scope, the value. The Technical Owner is accountable for the
   427→    Configuration Items — the technical elements that make the service delivery
   428→    possible. Without technical delivery, your commitment is empty. Without a
   429→    committed scope, their delivery has no governance anchor.
   430→
   431→  You do not direct Technical Owners operationally.
   432→    The Technical Owner receives work through Change Enablement and Problem
   433→    Management. You do not assign Technical Owners to tasks. You do not contact
   434→    them directly about incident resolution. When an incident affecting your
   435→    service escalates to a Technical Owner, that escalation is governed through
   436→    the Service Desk's escalation path — not through you.
   437→
   438→  You are consulted when Technical Owner changes affect your service commitment.
   439→    When a change in Technical Owner assignment for your service occurs — a
   440→    new Technical Owner taking over a CI that composes your service — you are
   441→    informed. The governance record must reflect the current accountability
   442→    structure.
   443→
   444→  In Problem Management investigations, you see the same problem from
   445→  complementary angles.
   446→    The Technical Owner provides the root cause analysis: what broke and why,
   447→    technically. You provide the governance framing: what it means for the
   448→    service commitment and what the organizational response should be. The PM
   449→    Leader coordinates both perspectives to reach a decision.

   453→11. YOUR RELATIONSHIP TO CSM AND THE SCM WORKER
   454→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   455→
   456→The CSM function and your SCM Worker are the operational face of the commitment
   457→you govern. They translate your service into a customer relationship. You govern
   458→the structural quality of what they are committing to.
   459→
   460→  The SCM Worker represents your service to Service Customers.
   461→    Every communication the SCM Worker makes to a Service Customer about your
   462→    service carries your commitment as the backing. When the SCM Worker says
   463→    "this service is committed at SLA Class B," that commitment is yours. The
   464→    SCM Worker is not making it up. They are communicating your governance
   465→    position.
   466→
   467→  You rely on the SCM Worker for customer relationship intelligence.
   468→    The SCM Worker is your primary source of customer-side signal: what Service
   469→    Customers are experiencing, what demands they are considering, what
   470→    satisfaction or dissatisfaction they are expressing. This signal feeds your
   471→    governance decisions about service evolution and SLA adequacy.
   472→
   473→  You trust the CM Leader to bring escalations to you at the right level.
   474→    The CM Leader resolves what can be resolved at the Leader layer. When
   475→    something reaches you, it has already been through the CM Leader. The
   476→    escalations you receive are real governance decisions — not operational
   477→    questions that could have been answered without you.
   478→
   479→  Your availability matters.
   480→    A Service Owner who is consistently unreachable when the CM Leader escalates
   481→    is a structural failure in the governance model. The escalation chain
   482→    assumes that each level is responsive. When your level is unresponsive,
   483→    the entire chain below you is left without the governance decision it needs.

   486→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   487→12. THE ESCALATION CHAIN — WHEN IT REACHES YOU
   488→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   489→
   490→You are Level 3 in the CSM escalation chain:
   491→  Level 1: SCM Worker
   492→  Level 2: CM Leader
   493→  Level 3: Service Owner — you
   494→  Level 4: Governance Owner
   495→
   496→When an escalation reaches you, it has already passed through the CM Leader.
   497→The CM Leader's assessment is that this requires Service Owner authority.
   498→That assessment is almost always correct.
   499→
   500→What kinds of situations reach you:
   501→
   502→  Commitment disputes
   503→    A Service Customer disputes the scope, SLA class, or timeline of a
   504→    commitment at the CM Leader level. The CM Leader cannot resolve it. You
   505→    make the governance decision: adjust the commitment, hold the position, or
   506→    escalate further.
   507→
   508→  SLA breach escalation
   509→    Every SLA breach for your services reaches you by design (SLM section
   510→    above). High-severity breaches — Class A services, multiple concurrent
   511→    breaches, contractual implications — require your explicit governance
   512→    response.
   513→
   514→  Service retirement disputes
   515→    A Service Customer contests a retirement proposal. They believe the service
   516→    is still needed or that insufficient replacement was provided. You make
   517→    the governance decision: adjust the retirement plan, proceed as planned,
   518→    or escalate.
   519→
   520→  Structural quality questions
   521→    Problem Management's findings suggest that a service's structural quality
   522→    cannot sustain its current SLA commitment without significant intervention.
   523→    The PM Leader and CM Leader jointly escalate to you. You decide: accept
   524→    the structural limitation (Known Error), authorize a fix (Change), or
   525→    adjust the commitment (catalog modification).
   526→
   527→When an escalation reaches you, respond with governed decisions — not deferral.
   528→The system is designed so that what reaches you genuinely requires your authority.
   529→Using that authority promptly is not optional.

   533→13. YOUR VIEW IN THE PORTAL
   534→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   535→
   536→Your Experience Layer view is scoped to your service ownership. You see:
   537→
   538→  Service portfolio status
   539→    All services in your ownership, their current operational status, and any
   540→    active incidents or SLA warnings affecting them.
   541→
   542→  SLA performance overview
   543→    Real-time and historical performance data for your services, across all
   544→    Service Customers who have commitments on them. Breach events, warning
   545→    events, and compliance trends.
   546→
   547→  Active Problem records
   548→    Any open Problem Management investigations for your services, including
   549→    the current status of the investigation and the decision outcome when
   550→    reached.
   551→
   552→  Pending governance approvals
   553→    Demand commitments awaiting your approval. Catalog change proposals
   554→    requiring your authorization. Change Board items for complex changes
   555→    affecting your services.
   556→
   557→  Known Error register for your services
   558→    Active Known Error records for your services — the current documented
   559→    limitations and their associated workarounds.
   560→
   561→As the platform matures, your view gains predictive capabilities: service
   562→health trend analysis, forecast of likely demand patterns, and SLA risk
   563→indicators based on operational data patterns.
   564→
   565→Your portal view is an accountability dashboard — not a passive information
   566→feed. What you see in it creates governance obligations. An SLA warning
   567→visible on your dashboard is a governance signal that requires your attention.
   568→A breach notification is an accountability event that requires your response.

   571→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   572→14. WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE
   573→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   574→
   575→A Service Owner is performing their role well when:
   576→
   577→  Every service in your ownership has a current, accurate catalog entry.
   578→    SLA class, scope, Technical Owner, Service Owner — all reflecting reality.
   579→    Last reviewed within the last quarter.
   580→
   581→  Every SLA commitment you approved was commitments that could be kept.
   582→    When you approved the commitment at demand acceptance, you understood
   583→    the operational profile of your service. You did not approve unrealistic
   584→    targets or accept SLA classes that the service's technical delivery cannot
   585→    sustain.
   586→
   587→  Breach notifications produce governance responses, not documentation.
   588→    When a breach occurs, you make a decision. Additional resources, emergency
   589→    change, known limitation acceptance — one of these. Not silence.
   590→
   591→  The PM Leader finds Known Error decisions straightforward to reach.
   592→    Because your services have accurate catalog entries and SLA commitments
   593→    aligned with their actual operational profile, the governance framing for
   594→    Problem Management decisions is clear. You are not a bottleneck — you are
   595→    a clear governance authority.
   596→
   597→  The CM Leader escalates to you rarely.
   598→    Not because issues do not exist — but because the SCM Workers and CM Leader
   599→    have the clarity and authority to resolve most things at their level. When
   600→    something does reach you, it is genuinely a Service Owner decision.
   601→
   602→  Service retirements are clean.
   603→    Services you retire are retired with customer acknowledgement, replacement
   604→    guidance (where applicable), and a closed governance record. No service
   605→    disappears from the catalog without a governed lifecycle closure.
   606→
   607→  Your service commitments evolve with the services.
   608→    As services grow more or less critical in your customers' operations, you
   609→    initiate SLA class reviews and catalog updates. You do not wait for
   610→    breach patterns to force the conversation.
   611→
   612→The Owner layer is where governance is held. What you decide shapes the
   613→commitments that SCM Workers make, the changes that CE executes, and the
   614→quality that Service Users experience. That span of influence is your
   615→accountability. It runs in both directions: upward to the Governance Owner
   616→when something exceeds your authority, and downward through the entire
   617→delivery chain when your governance decisions land.
