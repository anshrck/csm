    36→1.  WHO YOU ARE
    37→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    38→
    39→You are a Service Customer.
    40→
    41→In the CereBree uSMS model, a Service Customer is not an individual — it is
    42→an organizational unit. A business division. A department. A company entity.
    43→An external organization receiving managed IT services. What defines you as a
    44→Service Customer is not your personal title — it is your formal relationship
    45→with the IT organization.
    46→
    47→That relationship includes:
    48→
    49→  A service agreement
    50→    What has been committed to you: which services, at which service level,
    51→    for how many users, under which conditions. This is not aspirational. It
    52→    is a governance commitment — tracked, monitored, and reported.
    53→
    54→  Budget accountability
    55→    You are accountable for the IT spend your organizational unit consumes.
    56→    Services are not free. Demands have cost implications. SLA classes carry
    57→    resource implications. Your role includes governing how IT resources are
    58→    consumed by your organizational unit.
    59→
    60→  User governance
    61→    You define which services your users may access. No user in your unit
    62→    can access a service you have not authorized. This is not an
    63→    administrative technicality — it is your governance authority over
    64→    the services your team uses.
    65→
    66→  Escalation authority
    67→    When commitments are not met, you have the right — and the structural
    68→    backing — to hold the IT organization accountable. The SLA governance
    69→    model makes this concrete and governed, not political.
    70→
    71→In practice, one or more people in your organization act in the Service
    72→Customer role: a department lead, an IT liaison, a divisional manager. But
    73→regardless of who speaks on behalf of the Service Customer, the role is always
    74→at the organizational level, not the individual level.
    75→
    76→This distinction matters. It means your agreement with IT is formal, your
    77→commitments are tracked, and your services are governed — not managed by
    78→whoever happens to answer the phone this week.

    81→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    82→2.  YOUR NAMED CONTACT: THE SCM WORKER
    83→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    84→
    85→You have one named contact in the IT organization: your SCM Worker.
    86→
    87→SCM stands for Service Customer Manager. Your SCM Worker is not a helpdesk
    88→agent. They are not a ticket processor. They are your dedicated governance
    89→contact — the person who translates your business needs into governed IT
    90→decisions and who holds the IT organization accountable to your commitments.
    91→
    92→Your SCM Worker is your single thread of communication with the IT
    93→organization. When something changes, they tell you. When you need something,
    94→you tell them. When something goes wrong, they carry the governance response
    95→back to you. You do not deal with engineers, change teams, or technical owners
    96→directly — that is their job.
    97→
    98→What your SCM Worker does for you:
    99→
   100→  Receives and assesses your demands
   101→    When you need a new service, a modification to an existing one, or want
   102→    to retire a service your team no longer uses, you bring it to your SCM
   103→    Worker. They assess the demand against the service catalog, determine
   104→    whether it is already covered by an existing offering, and either redirect
   105→    you to a solution that already exists — or formally process your demand
   106→    through the IT organization.
   107→
   108→  Manages your service commitments
   109→    Your SCM Worker knows exactly which services have been committed to your
   110→    organizational unit, at which SLA class, and what the current compliance
   111→    status is. They work from governed records, not memory or informal notes.
   112→
   113→  Reports on SLA performance
   114→    You receive regular SLA performance reports. These are not marketing
   115→    summaries — they are governance reports: what was committed, what was
   116→    delivered, where there were breaches, and what is being done about them.
   117→    When there is a warning before a breach occurs, your SCM Worker alerts
   118→    you proactively. You are never the last to know.
   119→
   120→  Maintains your catalog view
   121→    Your SCM Worker ensures that the service catalog — as it applies to your
   122→    organizational unit — reflects your current agreements at all times. When
   123→    a service changes, they manage the process. When a service is retired,
   124→    they communicate it. Nothing appears in your catalog without governance
   125→    backing.
   126→
   127→  Is your contact throughout the entire demand lifecycle
   128→    Once you submit a demand, your SCM Worker stays with it until it is
   129→    fulfilled. Even when execution is handed to Change Enablement — the team
   130→    that builds and delivers the change — your SCM Worker retains
   131→    communication ownership. You receive progress, delays, and completion
   132→    confirmation through your SCM Worker. Never through engineers. Never
   133→    through a system message without context.
   134→
   135→This is not a convenience. It is a structural design decision. Every
   136→interaction between your organizational unit and the IT organization goes
   137→through a governed channel — one that creates records, applies process rules,
   138→and produces traceable outcomes.
   139→
   140→You do not have multiple contacts in IT. You have one: your SCM Worker.

   144→3.  WHAT YOU CAN DO
   145→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   146→
   147→3.1  Submit a Demand
   148→
   149→A Demand is a formal request for a change to the service landscape: a new
   150→service for your team, a modification to an existing service (scope, access,
   151→capacity, SLA class), or the retirement of a service you no longer need.
   152→
   153→Demands are submitted to your SCM Worker — not to a portal form alone, not to
   154→the Service Desk, and not to Technical Owners directly.
   155→
   156→The Demand lifecycle:
   157→
   158→  NEW
   159→    Your demand has been received and recorded. A Demand Record exists.
   160→
   161→  UNDER_REVIEW
   162→    Your SCM Worker is assessing the demand. They check whether it is already
   163→    covered by the service catalog. They evaluate technical feasibility and
   164→    portfolio alignment. They may ask you clarifying questions during this phase.
   165→
   166→  QUOTED
   167→    The IT organization has assessed your demand as feasible and not already
   168→    covered. Your SCM Worker presents you with a formal quote: scope, timeline,
   169→    SLA class, and resource or cost implications. You review it and decide.
   170→
   171→  ACCEPTED
   172→    You have approved the quote. Your demand transitions to Change Enablement
   173→    for technical execution. Your SCM Worker stays with it and keeps you
   174→    informed throughout.
   175→
   176→  IN_CHANGE
   177→    The technical work is underway. The change team is building, modifying, or
   178→    retiring the service as scoped. Your SCM Worker provides you with progress
   179→    updates at defined milestones.
   180→
   181→  FULFILLED
   182→    The service has been created, modified, or retired. The service catalog has
   183→    been updated to reflect the new reality. Your commitment is live.
   184→
   185→  CLOSED
   186→    The Demand Record is formally closed. The governance record is complete.
   187→
   188→If a demand is assessed as already covered by an existing offering, it is
   189→REDIRECTED — your SCM Worker informs you and points you to the relevant
   190→service or request type. If a demand is assessed as infeasible or outside the
   191→agreed scope, it is REJECTED — with a documented reason, communicated to you
   192→by your SCM Worker.
   193→
   194→A rejected or redirected demand is not a failure. It is the system working:
   195→you get an honest assessment, not a promise that cannot be kept.
   196→
   197→
   198→3.2  Approve or Reject a Quote
   199→
   200→When your SCM Worker issues a quote, you have the authority to accept or reject
   201→it. This is a formal governance decision.
   202→
   203→What a quote contains:
   204→
   205→  – The scope of the demand as understood and assessed by the IT organization
   206→  – Estimated effort and timeline
   207→  – The SLA class that will govern the resulting service or modification
   208→  – Any cost or resource implications
   209→  – Any dependencies or constraints that affect delivery
   210→
   211→If the quote matches your expectations, you accept it. Formal acceptance
   212→commits both parties: the IT organization to delivering what was quoted, you to
   213→the associated commitments. From this point forward, SLA monitoring applies.
   214→
   215→If the quote does not match your expectations — the scope is wrong, the
   216→timeline is unacceptable, the SLA class is not what you need — you reject it.
   217→Rejection returns the demand to your SCM Worker for reassessment or formal
   218→closure, with your documented reason. You are never required to accept a quote
   219→that does not serve your organizational needs.
   220→
   221→Your CM Leader (the leader of the CSM function) approves every quote before it
   222→reaches you. This means you are receiving a governance-validated commitment —
   223→not an estimate produced under pressure.
   224→
   225→
   226→3.3  Track Demand Status
   227→
   228→From the moment a demand is submitted, its status is visible to you in the
   229→portal. You do not need to ask your SCM Worker where your demand is — the
   230→system tells you.
   231→
   232→Your SCM Worker supplements this with direct communication at key transitions:
   233→when a quote is ready, when the demand enters change execution, when milestones
   234→are reached, and when fulfillment is confirmed.
   235→
   236→You are never in the dark about your demand's progress.
   237→
   238→
   239→3.4  Hold IT Accountable for SLA Commitments
   240→
   241→This is one of your most important rights as a Service Customer — and it is
   242→structurally enforced, not merely promised.
   243→
   244→Every service committed to your organizational unit carries an SLA class. That
   245→class defines the response and resolution targets that apply when things go
   246→wrong, and the fulfillment targets that apply when requests are made.
   247→
   248→The SLA monitoring system watches these targets continuously. When a target is
   249→approaching breach — at 80% of the clock consumed — you are notified via your
   250→SCM Worker, proactively, before the breach occurs. When a breach happens at
   251→100%, you are notified immediately, with context: what happened, which service,
   252→what is being done.
   253→
   254→You receive a regular SLA performance report for your service portfolio:
   255→  – SLA compliance per service, per period
   256→  – Any breaches that occurred, with root cause context where available
   257→  – The status of any ongoing remediation
   258→
   259→If you identify a pattern of SLA underperformance, you have the right to
   260→escalate. Your SCM Worker escalates to the CM Leader. The CM Leader escalates
   261→to the Service Owner. This chain is defined, named, and fast.
   262→
   263→The system is built to make poor performance visible — to you and to the IT
   264→organization simultaneously. SLA governance is not a courtesy. It is a
   265→structural commitment.
   266→
   267→
   268→3.5  Govern What Your Users Can Access
   269→
   270→Your organizational unit is the governance authority for the services your
   271→users consume. No user in your unit can access a service or request type that
   272→your Service Customer agreement does not cover.
   273→
   274→This entitlement model works as follows:
   275→
   276→  Your agreement with the IT organization defines which services are active for
   277→  your unit and at which level. These are your entitlements.
   278→
   279→  Your users inherit those entitlements by virtue of belonging to your
   280→  organizational unit. They can request and receive what your agreement covers.
   281→  They cannot request or receive what it does not cover — and the system
   282→  enforces this automatically.
   283→
   284→  More specific assignments override broader ones. If a particular user within
   285→  your unit needs access that differs from the unit default, that can be
   286→  assigned individually — still within the governed model, still traceable.
   287→
   288→When you submit a demand for a new service or a scope change, you are
   289→exercising your governance authority over this entitlement structure. When a
   290→user tells you they cannot access something, the answer is either in the
   291→existing entitlement model (they are not entitled) or in a demand to expand
   292→the scope — which you initiate as Service Customer.

   296→4.  WHAT YOU CANNOT DO — AND WHY
   297→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   298→
   299→The following are structural boundaries — not policies that can be waived.
   300→
   301→  You cannot access the Service Desk.
   302→    The Service Desk serves Service Users — individuals using services in their
   303→    daily work. Its four processes (Incident Management, Request Fulfillment,
   304→    Access Management, and Event Management) are for operational service
   305→    interactions, not for organizational governance. If something in your unit
   306→    is not working, the affected user reports it via the Service Desk. If you
   307→    need a new or modified service, that comes through Customer Service
   308→    Management and your SCM Worker.
   309→
   310→  You cannot contact Technical Owners directly.
   311→    Technical Owners are reachable only through defined governance paths:
   312→    Change Enablement and Problem Management. Direct contact bypasses the
   313→    governance model, creates undocumented commitments, and consumes the
   314→    Technical Owner's attention outside any governed structure. If you need
   315→    something from a Technical Owner's domain, it flows through your SCM
   316→    Worker and the appropriate process.
   317→
   318→  You cannot report incidents.
   319→    Incidents are reported by Service Users through the Service Desk. If a
   320→    service your unit depends on is disrupted, your users report it through
   321→    their Service Desk access. Your SCM Worker is informed through SLA
   322→    monitoring when those incidents approach or breach their targets.
   323→
   324→  You cannot approve service changes.
   325→    Change approval authority belongs to the Change Enablement function. You
   326→    accept or reject quotes — but the execution and technical approval of
   327→    changes is governed by the CE Leader, not by you. The same person cannot
   328→    both request a change and approve its execution. This separation exists
   329→    by design to protect the governance integrity of the system.
   330→
   331→  You cannot view other customers' service data.
   332→    Tenant isolation is structural. Your service portfolio, SLA performance
   333→    data, demand history, and user entitlements are visible only to you and
   334→    to the IT organization acting on your behalf. No other Service Customer
   335→    can see your data. This applies equally in both internal and B2B contexts.
   336→
   337→  You cannot modify the service catalog directly.
   338→    The catalog is modified through Change Enablement only. CSM triggers and
   339→    oversees catalog changes. Change Enablement executes them. This separation
   340→    exists because a catalog that can be edited informally is a catalog that
   341→    cannot be trusted.
   342→
   343→These boundaries are not bureaucratic obstacles. They are the structural design
   344→that makes your service relationship governable, your commitments enforceable,
   345→and your data protected. Every boundary that applies to you also protects you.

   348→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   349→5.  YOUR SERVICE PORTFOLIO
   350→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   351→
   352→Your service portfolio is the set of services that have been committed to your
   353→organizational unit and are currently active. It is maintained in the uSeC
   354→Service Catalog and governed through Service Catalog Management.
   355→
   356→The catalog describes each service through nine structured perspectives: what
   357→it does, who it serves, what SLA level has been committed, what support levels
   358→apply, what can be requested from it, who owns its quality, who delivers it
   359→technically, whether it is a commodity or a custom offering, and which service
   360→package it belongs to.
   361→
   362→This means you always know what you have agreed to. Nothing in your portfolio
   363→exists without a governed description. The catalog is not a marketing brochure.
   364→It is your governance record of what IT has committed to deliver.
   365→
   366→Service Packages
   367→Your services may be organized into service packages — logical bundles of
   368→related services designed for coherent consumption. A standard employee
   369→onboarding package might include identity provisioning, device setup, email
   370→configuration, and network access as a single governed bundle. Each service
   371→within the package retains its own lifecycle, owner, and SLA — but the package
   372→simplifies how you govern the overall relationship.
   373→
   374→When a service in your portfolio changes — because you submitted a demand,
   375→because a technical change was required, or because the catalog was updated —
   376→your SCM Worker communicates this to you before and after the change. The
   377→catalog is always current. You are always informed.

   380→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   381→6.  SLA CLASSES: WHAT WAS COMMITTED TO YOU
   382→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   383→
   384→Every service in your portfolio is assigned an SLA class. There are four:
   385→
   386→  Class A — Mission Critical
   387→    For services where any outage has immediate, significant business impact
   388→    and no acceptable alternative exists while the service is down. Highest
   389→    availability targets. Fastest incident response and resolution commitments.
   390→
   391→  Class B — Business Important
   392→    For services important to normal operations but where brief disruption can
   393→    be managed with workarounds. Strong reliability. Defined response and
   394→    resolution windows. Core supporting services.
   395→
   396→  Class C — Standard Operational
   397→    For services with predictable, manageable impact when disrupted. Standard
   398→    response and resolution windows. Services that support work without stopping
   399→    it when disrupted.
   400→
   401→  Class D — Advisory / Best Effort
   402→    For consulting, exploratory, or low-criticality services. No committed
   403→    clock-based response or resolution targets at the Minimal Stage. Best-effort
   404→    delivery model.
   405→
   406→The SLA class for each service in your portfolio was established when your
   407→demand was accepted. It is documented in the catalog and enforced through the
   408→SLA monitoring system.
   409→
   410→If the committed class no longer matches the actual criticality of the service
   411→in your operations — for example, a service has grown more business-critical
   412→than originally anticipated — that is a conversation to have with your SCM
   413→Worker. It may lead to a catalog modification demand, processed through the
   414→normal governance path.
   415→
   416→SLA classes are not marketing tiers. They carry concrete commitments that the
   417→IT organization is monitored against continuously. A breach triggers immediate
   418→governance response — not a post-hoc explanation.

   422→7.  YOUR VIEW IN THE PORTAL
   423→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   424→
   425→The CereBree Experience Layer is your governed entry point into the system.
   426→One URL. One login. One view of everything relevant to your role and scope.
   427→
   428→What you see is governed by your entitlements. You see exactly what is relevant
   429→to your Service Customer scope — nothing more, nothing less. If data is not in
   430→your scope, it is not present in your view. This is not a permission filter
   431→applied on top of a shared dataset. It is entitlement-based rendering.
   432→
   433→What your portal view contains:
   434→
   435→  Your service portfolio
   436→    All services committed to your organizational unit, their current status,
   437→    SLA class, and relevant catalog information.
   438→
   439→  SLA performance dashboard
   440→    Real-time and historical performance data for your services. Which services
   441→    are within target. Which carry active warnings. Which have experienced
   442→    breaches and what the current remediation status is.
   443→
   444→  Demand pipeline
   445→    All active and recent demands in their current lifecycle state. What is
   446→    under review. What has been quoted and is awaiting your decision. What is
   447→    in change execution. What has been fulfilled.
   448→
   449→  Notifications and alerts
   450→    SLA warnings, breach notifications, demand status changes, and catalog
   451→    updates. Every notification is backed by a governance event. Nothing appears
   452→    without cause.
   453→
   454→  Your recent activity
   455→    A cross-domain aggregation of your service-related activity: demands
   456→    submitted, quotes reviewed, services activated or retired.
   457→
   458→As the platform matures through the stage model, your portal view gains
   459→additional capabilities: analytics, usage and cost transparency, demand
   460→forecasting, and deeper SLA performance insight. At every stage, the
   461→principle is the same: you see what is relevant, in real time, without
   462→having to ask.

   466→8.  COGNI: YOUR AI ASSISTANT
   467→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   468→
   469→Cogni is the CereBree AI Assistant. It is available to you within the portal,
   470→within your governed scope and entitlement boundaries. Cogni does not operate
   471→outside those boundaries. It does not access other customers' data. It does
   472→not make commitments on behalf of the IT organization.
   473→
   474→What Cogni does for you as a Service Customer:
   475→
   476→  Smart Stage
   477→    – SLA performance at a glance: "Your top 3 services by SLA compliance
   478→      this month: Email (99.8%), ERP (98.2%), Data Warehouse (94.1% —
   479→      one breach recorded this period)."
   480→    – Demand status: "Your demand for the collaboration platform is currently
   481→      in Change Enablement. Status: Planning. Expected completion: 12 April."
   482→    – Breach communication drafting: when an SLA breach occurs, Cogni prepares
   483→      the initial communication for your SCM Worker to review and send — with
   484→      root cause context from Problem Management included where available.
   485→
   486→  Mature Stage
   487→    – Demand forecasting: based on usage patterns and demand history, Cogni
   488→      identifies capacity or service gaps before they become demands. Your SCM
   489→      Worker is notified to discuss proactively.
   490→    – Usage and cost transparency: a breakdown of service consumption against
   491→      your committed baseline, by service.
   492→    – Commitment evolution guidance: when SLA performance patterns suggest a
   493→      service is under-committed (e.g., Class B with consistent Class A
   494→      thresholds), Cogni surfaces this for your SCM Worker to discuss.
   495→
   496→What Cogni never does:
   497→
   498→  – Surface another customer's service data or SLA performance
   499→  – Make or modify service commitments without SCM Worker and CM Leader approval
   500→  – Communicate SLA breaches to you without SCM Worker review
   501→  – Replace a governance decision with an automated output
   502→
   503→Cogni is an intelligence layer, not a decision layer. Accountability stays with
   504→your SCM Worker, the CM Leader, and the Service Owner. Cogni makes them faster
   505→and better informed — it does not replace their judgment.

   509→9.  ESCALATION PATHS
   510→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   511→
   512→When something requires escalation beyond your normal SCM Worker interaction,
   513→the escalation path is defined, named, and fast.
   514→
   515→  Level 1 — SCM Worker
   516→    First contact for all governance topics: demand questions, SLA concerns,
   517→    catalog questions, status updates, quote negotiations.
   518→
   519→  Level 2 — CM Leader (Customer Management Leader)
   520→    If your SCM Worker cannot resolve an issue, or if you need to escalate
   521→    a dispute about a quote, rejection, or SLA commitment, the CM Leader is
   522→    the next authority. The CM Leader holds operational accountability for the
   523→    entire CSM function.
   524→
   525→  Level 3 — Service Owner
   526→    If a dispute involves the quality, scope, or SLA performance of a specific
   527→    service, the Service Owner is the accountability holder for that service.
   528→    The CM Leader escalates to the Service Owner when required.
   529→
   530→  Level 4 — Governance Owner
   531→    In cases where service governance decisions affect organizational or
   532→    contractual commitments at the highest level, the Governance Owner holds
   533→    ultimate authority. This level involves decisions affecting the structural
   534→    integrity of the service relationship.
   535→
   536→You do not navigate these escalation levels yourself. Your SCM Worker manages
   537→the escalation process on your behalf. Your role is to communicate clearly
   538→what the issue is and what resolution you need.
   539→
   540→In a B2B context — where you are an external organization receiving managed
   541→IT services — these escalation paths carry contractual weight. A breach that
   542→is an internal governance matter at Level 2 may be a contractual accountability
   543→matter at Level 3 or 4.

   546→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   547→10. YOUR RELATIONSHIP TO THE BACK-OFFICE
   548→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   549→
   550→Behind your SCM Worker and the visible processes of Customer Service
   551→Management, the uSMS Back-Office operates continuously. You do not interact
   552→with it directly. That is the point.
   553→
   554→  Problem Management
   555→    When a service in your portfolio experiences recurring failures, Problem
   556→    Management investigates the structural root cause. Its job is not to close
   557→    tickets — it is to make a proportionate governance decision: fix the
   558→    structural issue permanently (triggering a change), document it as a known
   559→    limitation with a workaround, or accept it. When Problem Management's
   560→    findings are relevant to your SLA commitments, your SCM Worker communicates
   561→    them to you with the context you need to understand what is being done
   562→    and why.
   563→
   564→  Change Enablement
   565→    All structural changes to your services — new services, modifications,
   566→    retirements — are executed by Change Enablement. CE is the only function
   567→    authorized to write to the service catalog. When your demand transitions
   568→    to IN_CHANGE, Change Enablement is the execution authority. You do not
   569→    interact with CE directly. Your SCM Worker does.
   570→
   571→  Service Configuration Management
   572→    Technical Owners maintain the current state of the configuration items
   573→    that compose your services. This data makes incident investigations,
   574→    change assessments, and root cause analyses accurate. When your SCM
   575→    Worker reports on a service change, the configuration data is the
   576→    technical foundation of that report.
   577→
   578→  Knowledge Management
   579→    Every incident resolution, every root cause analysis, every known
   580→    workaround is governed knowledge within the system. The Known Error
   581→    Database means recurring issues are surfaced immediately when they recur
   582→    — rather than being rediscovered each time. This benefits your users
   583→    directly: faster incident resolution, more consistent service behavior.
   584→
   585→The Back-Office is invisible to you — and that invisibility is a feature,
   586→not a gap. It means the IT organization handles structural complexity so that
   587→your interaction remains clean, governed, and focused on what matters to you.

   590→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   591→11. WHAT HAPPENS WHEN THINGS GO WRONG
   592→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   593→
   594→Services fail. Commitments are sometimes missed. The uSMS system does not
   595→promise perfection — it promises governance.
   596→
   597→When a service your unit depends on is disrupted:
   598→  Your users report the disruption through the Service Desk. The Service Desk
   599→  categorizes the incident, checks for known errors, applies workarounds where
   600→  available, and works to restore service. SLA monitoring tracks the incident
   601→  against the committed targets for your service's SLA class.
   602→
   603→  If the incident approaches breach (80% of the SLA clock consumed), your SCM
   604→  Worker is notified and alerts you proactively.
   605→
   606→  If a breach occurs, you are notified immediately with full context: what
   607→  happened, which service, what is being done. The Service Owner for the
   608→  affected service is also notified. Governance action is triggered — not just
   609→  explained after the fact.
   610→
   611→When a service repeatedly fails in the same way:
   612→  If the same service generates three or more incidents within seven days,
   613→  Problem Management is automatically triggered to investigate the structural
   614→  root cause. You do not need to escalate. The system detects the pattern and
   615→  governs it.
   616→
   617→  Problem Management produces a decision: fix the structural issue (triggering
   618→  a change), document it as a known limitation (with a workaround accessible
   619→  to the Service Desk), or accept it as proportionate. You receive the outcome
   620→  from your SCM Worker with context — including what the decision means for
   621→  your SLA commitments going forward.
   622→
   623→When a committed service is not delivered on time:
   624→  Demand fulfillment adherence is part of your SCM Worker's accountability.
   625→  If a demand's committed timeline is not met, your SCM Worker provides you
   626→  with an updated timeline and a documented reason. This is governed
   627→  communication — not an informal apology.
   628→
   629→When SLA performance is persistently below what was committed:
   630→  Your SCM Worker and CM Leader have the governance tools to respond. Problem
   631→  Management investigation, change authorization, escalation to the Service
   632→  Owner — all are available. Your role is to ensure the governance system
   633→  knows what you are experiencing. The structural response is the IT
   634→  organization's responsibility.
   635→
   636→The governance model does not make failures disappear. It makes them visible,
   637→attributed, and actioned. That is the difference between a service relationship
   638→and a service conversation.
