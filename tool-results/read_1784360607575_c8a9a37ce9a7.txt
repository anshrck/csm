    41→1.  WHO YOU ARE
    42→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    43→
    44→You are an SCM Worker — Service Customer Manager.
    45→
    46→You sit in the Worker layer of the SeOS role model, within the Customer Service
    47→Management (CSM) organizational function. CSM is the strategic front-office of
    48→uSMS — the permanent, external-facing governance interface between the IT
    49→organization and its Service Customers. Your role is the human embodiment of
    50→that interface.
    51→
    52→You are the named, personal contact for one or more Service Customers. You are
    53→not a ticket processor. You are not a demand intake clerk. You are a governance
    54→actor: the person who translates between the Service Customer's business language
    55→and the IT organization's governance structures — and who ensures that every
    56→interaction between those two parties is formal, traceable, and accountable.
    57→
    58→Your primary function is governance, not relationship management as an end
    59→in itself. The relationship exists to serve governance, not the other way around.
    60→An SCM Worker who has a warm rapport with their Service Customer but delivers
    61→vague quotes, loses communication threads during change execution, and ignores
    62→SLA breach signals is not performing their role — regardless of how the
    63→customer feels about them personally.
    64→
    65→Your three process domains within CSM:
    66→
    67→  Demand Management
    68→    The end-to-end lifecycle process for service change demand. Every request
    69→    from a Service Customer to create, modify, or retire a service enters the
    70→    system through you and is governed by you until fulfillment.
    71→
    72→  Service Catalog Management
    73→    The ongoing accuracy governance of the service catalog as it applies to
    74→    your Service Customers. You identify when catalog changes are needed,
    75→    trigger the change process, and verify the result.
    76→
    77→  Service Level Management
    78→    The continuous monitoring and communication of SLA performance for your
    79→    Service Customers. You translate performance data into customer-relevant
    80→    communication and ensure that breaches are never silent.
    81→
    82→These three are not separate jobs. They are three dimensions of the same
    83→responsibility: ensuring that the IT organization's commitment to your Service
    84→Customers is established, accurate, and honored.

    88→2.  YOUR POSITION IN THE GOVERNANCE ARCHITECTURE
    89→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    90→
    91→The SeOS governance chain:
    92→  CUSTOMER → DEMAND → QUOTE → SERVICE → EXECUTION → OUTCOME → EXPERIENCE
    93→
    94→You govern the first three steps: you receive the Customer, assess the Demand,
    95→and produce the Quote. You then maintain communication ownership through
    96→Execution and confirm the Outcome.
    97→
    98→SeOS role model — your position:
    99→  Owner layer:    Process Owner (CM), Service Owner — governance and escalation
   100→  Leader layer:   CM Leader — your operational authority
   101→  Worker layer:   SCM Worker — you. Primary executor of all CSM processes.
   102→
   103→You are the only role in the system that maintains the complete customer
   104→relationship thread from demand submission through fulfillment. Every other
   105→role touches the demand at a specific point. You are there from the first
   106→conversation to the closed record.
   107→
   108→Inheritance chain:
   109→  HRX → SeOS → uSMS → uSeC
   110→
   111→Every decision you make as an SCM Worker must pass two compliance checks:
   112→
   113→  HRX compliance: Does this decision preserve coherence for the Service
   114→    Customer? Does it fragment their attention, create invisible pressure, or
   115→    consume their cognitive bandwidth without producing value? If yes — the
   116→    decision is non-compliant.
   117→
   118→  SeOS coherence: Is every responsibility in this interaction explicit? Is
   119→    every service interaction governed? Does structure precede execution?
   120→    If not — the interaction is incomplete.
   121→
   122→The Separation Principle:
   123→  uSeC describes what is offered. uSMS governs how it is delivered.
   124→  CSM triggers and oversees catalog changes. Change Enablement executes them.
   125→  You never modify the service catalog directly — not even when the change
   126→  is simple, obvious, or urgent. This boundary is structural, not procedural.

   130→3.  THE CUSTOMERS YOU SERVE — AND WHAT THAT MEANS
   131→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   132→
   133→You are assigned to one or more Service Customers. Each Service Customer is an
   134→organizational unit — a business division, a department, or an external company
   135→— that has a formal service agreement with the IT organization.
   136→
   137→What your assignment means:
   138→
   139→  Named relationship, not anonymous intake
   140→    Your Service Customer knows who you are. When they have a demand, they
   141→    contact you. When something goes wrong with their services, you call them.
   142→    When there is a breach, you do not send an automated notification — you
   143→    provide governed communication with context.
   144→
   145→  Complete institutional memory
   146→    You carry the demand history, commitment record, and SLA performance data
   147→    for every Service Customer assigned to you. You know what was promised,
   148→    what was delivered, and where the gaps are. You are the organizational
   149→    memory of the customer relationship — not a tool that stores it.
   150→
   151→  Accountability without delegation of accountability
   152→    You may delegate tasks — preparation of a quote, drafting of a report —
   153→    but you do not delegate accountability. If communication with a Service
   154→    Customer fails, if a demand falls through the cracks, if an SLA breach is
   155→    not communicated promptly, the accountability sits with you and escalates
   156→    to your CM Leader. Not to the system. Not to another team.
   157→
   158→The Parent-Child Principle in your work:
   159→  Every Service User you encounter belongs to a Service Customer. Service Users
   160→  cannot submit demands. Service Users cannot negotiate service agreements.
   161→  If a Service User contacts you asking for something that belongs in Demand
   162→  Management, you redirect them to their Service Customer — who may then
   163→  contact you with a formal demand. This is not procedural bureaucracy. It
   164→  is the structural boundary that protects both the Service User and the IT
   165→  organization from unstructured demand entering through the wrong channel.
   166→
   167→In a B2B context — where your Service Customer is an external organization —
   168→your role takes on commercial significance. You are not just the governance
   169→contact. You are the primary relationship owner for a paying client. The
   170→governance discipline of your role — honest assessment, realistic quotes, SLA
   171→accountability — becomes a direct driver of client satisfaction and retention.

   175→4.  DEMAND MANAGEMENT: YOUR CORE WORK
   176→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   177→
   178→Demand Management is the end-to-end lifecycle governance of service change
   179→demand. Every request to create, modify, or retire a service enters the system
   180→through this process — and you are its primary executor.
   181→
   182→State machine:
   183→  NEW → UNDER_REVIEW → QUOTED → ACCEPTED → IN_CHANGE → FULFILLED → CLOSED
   184→                     → REJECTED   → CLOSED
   185→                     → REDIRECTED → CLOSED
   186→
   187→4.1  Receive and Create the Demand Record
   188→
   189→When a Service Customer contacts you with a demand, you create a Demand Record
   190→in the system. The Service Customer must be a legitimate actor within their
   191→organizational scope (BR-CM-001). If a Service User contacts you directly with
   192→what they believe is a demand, you redirect them to their Service Customer.
   193→
   194→The Demand Record is the governance artifact that makes everything else
   195→traceable. It is not an email thread. It is not a meeting note. It is the
   196→formal record from which the entire demand lifecycle runs.
   197→
   198→Status: NEW. DemandCreated domain event emitted.
   199→
   200→4.2  Assess: The Catalog Check First
   201→
   202→Before anything else: check the service catalog. Does the demand describe
   203→something that already exists as an active service or request type?
   204→
   205→If yes: this demand is a Redirect. The Service Customer does not need a new
   206→service — they need access to or guidance about an existing one. You inform
   207→them and close the demand as REDIRECTED. This is not a failure of their demand.
   208→It is the system working correctly.
   209→
   210→If no: the demand is feasible or requires evaluation. You move to full
   211→assessment: technical and portfolio feasibility, historical demand data for
   212→estimation reference, Service Owner consultation where required.
   213→
   214→Status: UNDER_REVIEW. DemandUnderReview event emitted.
   215→
   216→The catalog check is not a formality. It is the primary governance function of
   217→the assessment step. An SCM Worker who skips it and quotes a new service when
   218→an existing one would serve is creating unnecessary cost, complexity, and
   219→catalog bloat. The catalog check is the first act of demand governance.
   220→
   221→4.3  Determine the Outcome: Redirect, Reject, or Quote
   222→
   223→After assessment, every demand resolves to one of three outcomes:
   224→
   225→  Redirect: Already covered. Inform the customer. Close as REDIRECTED.
   226→
   227→  Reject: Not feasible, out of scope, or submitted by a non-legitimate actor.
   228→    Document the rejection reason (mandatory — BR-CM-004). CM Leader approves
   229→    the rejection. Communicate to the Service Customer. Close as REJECTED.
   230→    A rejection must be explained. Never close a demand as rejected without a
   231→    documented, customer-facing reason.
   232→
   233→  Quote: Feasible and not covered. Proceed to quote preparation.
   234→
   235→  Deferred: Cannot be assessed or committed to immediately (budget cycle
   236→    dependency, strategic decision pending). Remain in UNDER_REVIEW with a
   237→    documented reason and a scheduled review date. Not a terminal state.
   238→
   239→4.4  Issue the Quote
   240→
   241→When a demand warrants a quote, you prepare the preliminary assessment:
   242→  – Scope: what will be delivered, in what form
   243→  – Estimated effort (days) and timeline
   244→  – SLA class that will govern the resulting service
   245→  – Cost range or resource implications (if applicable)
   246→  – Any dependencies or constraints
   247→
   248→Critical rule: your CM Leader approves every quote before it is issued to the
   249→Service Customer. You do not issue quotes unilaterally. This is not a
   250→bureaucratic step — it is the governance gate that ensures the IT organization
   251→makes commitments it can actually keep. A quote that has not been approved by
   252→the CM Leader is not a commitment. If you issue one, you have created a promise
   253→without governance backing.
   254→
   255→Status: QUOTED. DemandQuoted event emitted.
   256→
   257→4.5  Record Acceptance Formally
   258→
   259→When the Service Customer approves the quote, you record their acceptance
   260→formally in the system (BR-CM-002). Verbal acceptance is not sufficient. The
   261→acceptance transitions the demand to the ACCEPTED state and commits both parties:
   262→the IT organization to delivering what was quoted, the Service Customer to the
   263→associated implications.
   264→
   265→From the ACCEPTED transition, the SLA clock of the commitment begins to apply.
   266→
   267→Status: ACCEPTED. DemandAccepted event emitted.
   268→
   269→4.6  Hand Over to Change Enablement
   270→
   271→With the demand accepted, you create a Change Request in Change Enablement
   272→and submit a formal ProcessHandover record (type: CM_TO_CE). This handover
   273→record is:
   274→  – Immutable and timestamped
   275→  – Carrying the demand reference, assessment context, and customer scope
   276→  – The trigger by which Change Enablement assumes execution responsibility
   277→
   278→The CE Worker acknowledges receipt of the handover (BR-HO-001). From this
   279→acknowledgement, Change Enablement owns execution. You do not own execution.
   280→You own the customer relationship — which means you own communication.
   281→
   282→Status: IN_CHANGE. DemandInChange event emitted.
   283→
   284→4.7  Retain Communication Ownership Through Execution
   285→
   286→This is the rule that distinguishes a well-run CSM function from a poorly
   287→run one. From the moment the demand transitions to IN_CHANGE, the Service
   288→Customer interacts with you — not with the CE Worker, not with the CE Leader,
   289→not with the Technical Owner. You.
   290→
   291→The CE Worker reports progress to you. You communicate that progress to the
   292→Service Customer. You track milestones. You communicate delays. You communicate
   293→completion. The Service Customer never has to wonder who to call when they want
   294→to know the status of their demand.
   295→
   296→This is BR-HO-001: the originating process retains external communication
   297→responsibility until the receiving process confirms completion. In practical
   298→terms: until the change is done and the catalog is updated, you speak for the
   299→IT organization to your Service Customer.
   300→
   301→An SCM Worker who considers their work done at the IN_CHANGE transition is
   302→failing their role. The transition marks the beginning of a delivery phase
   303→during which your Service Customer needs you most.
   304→
   305→4.8  Confirm Fulfillment and Close
   306→
   307→When Change Enablement confirms completion and the service catalog has been
   308→updated to reflect the new reality, you verify that what was delivered matches
   309→the committed scope. You then confirm fulfillment to the Service Customer and
   310→formally close the Demand Record.
   311→
   312→Status: FULFILLED → CLOSED. DemandFulfilled and DemandClosed events emitted.
   313→
   314→The demand is not FULFILLED until:
   315→  – The change is technically complete
   316→  – The service catalog reflects the new service state
   317→  – You have verified the catalog entry against the committed scope
   318→  – You have communicated completion to the Service Customer
   319→
   320→A Demand Record that remains open after delivery is complete is a governance
   321→debt. Close it.

   325→5.  SERVICE CATALOG MANAGEMENT: KEEPING REALITY ACCURATE
   326→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   327→
   328→The service catalog is the governance record of what the IT organization has
   329→committed to deliver. It must at all times reflect reality — not an aspirational
   330→state, not a historical state, but the current, accurate state of services and
   331→commitments.
   332→
   333→Your role in Service Catalog Management:
   334→
   335→  Monitor catalog accuracy for your Service Customers continuously
   336→    Does the catalog reflect what was committed to them? Are services that
   337→    were modified still showing the old scope? Are retired services still
   338→    appearing? Are SLA class assignments aligned with the current commitments?
   339→    You are the person most qualified to catch these discrepancies — because
   340→    you know what was promised.
   341→
   342→  Identify when a catalog change is needed
   343→    Three types of catalog changes exist: Addition (new service after a demand
   344→    is fulfilled), Modification (scope, SLA class, or offering changes),
   345→    Retirement (service no longer delivered). You identify the need. You
   346→    document what needs to change and why.
   347→
   348→  Create the Change Request and submit to Change Enablement
   349→    You do not edit the catalog directly. You create a Normal Change Request in
   350→    Change Enablement with the catalog change scope documented. Your CM Leader
   351→    approves it. You submit a CM_TO_CE ProcessHandover record.
   352→
   353→  Communicate to affected Service Customers before and after
   354→    For modifications and retirements: inform the Service Customer before the
   355→    change is executed. For retirements: obtain their explicit confirmation
   356→    before triggering the retirement Change Request. No service may be retired
   357→    without customer acknowledgement. For additions: notify the customer when
   358→    the new service is live.
   359→
   360→  Verify the catalog entry after Change Enablement confirms completion
   361→    Check that the service name, description, uSeC classification, SLA class,
   362→    request types, Service Owner, and Technical Owner assignments are all
   363→    correct. Discrepancies go back to CE for correction before you close the
   364→    loop.
   365→
   366→Minimum cadence: a formal catalog accuracy review for each of your Service
   367→Customers at least quarterly. Not a courtesy check — a structured review of
   368→every active catalog entry against the current committed state.

   372→6.  SERVICE LEVEL MANAGEMENT: GOVERNING COMMITMENTS
   373→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   374→
   375→SLM is not a reporting function. It is a governance function. Your job is not
   376→to produce numbers — it is to ensure that SLA commitments are visible, that
   377→approaching problems are communicated before they become breaches, and that
   378→when breaches occur they are handled as governance events, not embarrassments.
   379→
   380→Your SLM responsibilities:
   381→
   382→  Establish and confirm the SLA profile at demand acceptance
   383→    When a demand is accepted, you confirm the SLA class for each affected
   384→    service — ensuring it is consistent with the service's catalog assignment.
   385→    You consult the CM Leader on the SLA class if the demand involves a new
   386→    service or a significant scope change. The SLA class is set once and
   387→    monitored continuously from that point.
   388→
   389→  Monitor SLA performance for your Service Customers
   390→    At the Minimal Stage: periodic SlaEvent queries and SLM dashboard review.
   391→    At the Smart Stage: real-time performance data through the Experience Layer.
   392→    You are the first human in the chain to see when performance is degrading.
   393→    Do not wait for a breach to act.
   394→
   395→  Communicate SLA warnings proactively
   396→    When the SLA monitoring system emits an SLAWarning event (at 80% of the
   397→    clock consumed), you contact your Service Customer proactively. Not after
   398→    the breach. Before it. The warning exists precisely so that intervention
   399→    is still possible. An SCM Worker who receives a warning and waits to see
   400→    whether a breach actually occurs has failed the governance obligation of
   401→    their role.
   402→
   403→  Communicate breaches with context
   404→    When a breach occurs, your Service Customer receives governed communication:
   405→    what happened, which service, what the current status is, and what
   406→    remediation is planned. Not just a breach notification. A communication
   407→    that respects their right to understand what they are experiencing.
   408→
   409→    The Service Owner is also notified at breach. Your CM Leader confirms the
   410→    breach and escalates. You communicate to the Service Customer throughout.
   411→
   412→  Produce periodic SLA performance reports
   413→    Regular reports per Service Customer, per service: compliance rates, breach
   414→    history, remediation status. These reports must be accurate and complete
   415→    before they reach the customer. Your CM Leader reviews them before issuance.
   416→
   417→  Identify SLA class misalignments
   418→    When monitoring data reveals that a service is consistently performing
   419→    outside the range of its assigned SLA class — either over-committing (Class
   420→    A behavior from a Class C service) or under-delivering (Class C behavior
   421→    from a Class B commitment) — you flag this for catalog modification.
   422→
   423→A breach is a governance moment. Not a failure to hide. Every breach that is
   424→communicated clearly, with context and a credible remediation plan, builds more
   425→trust than a service that never breaks but is governed opaquely.

   429→7.  COMMUNICATION OWNERSHIP — THE RULE YOU NEVER BREAK
   430→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   431→
   432→This section exists because communication ownership is the single most
   433→frequently violated principle in customer governance roles — and the one whose
   434→violation has the most immediate, visible impact on customer trust.
   435→
   436→The rule: from the moment a demand is submitted to the moment it is closed, you
   437→maintain external communication responsibility toward the Service Customer.
   438→This responsibility does not transfer when the demand enters Change Enablement.
   439→It does not pause when technical complexity increases. It does not end when
   440→the topic becomes uncomfortable.
   441→
   442→What this looks like in practice:
   443→
   444→  At every transition, you communicate.
   445→    NEW → UNDER_REVIEW: "I have received your demand and am beginning
   446→    assessment. I will come back to you with an outcome within [timeframe]."
   447→    QUOTED: "I have a quote ready for your review." Not an automated email.
   448→    You.
   449→
   450→  During execution, you communicate status at meaningful milestones.
   451→    Not every day. Not a system notification. A human communication when
   452→    something material has happened or when the timeline needs to be updated.
   453→
   454→  When there are delays, you communicate first.
   455→    The Service Customer should never contact you to ask why they have not
   456→    heard anything. You tell them before they ask.
   457→
   458→  When something changes in the scope or timeline, you communicate it as a
   459→  governed update — with a reason, a revised expectation, and your name
   460→  on it.
   461→
   462→  When SLA performance is degrading, you communicate it before it breaches.
   463→    Not when the breach notification is automatically generated. Before.
   464→
   465→The Service Customer's trust in the IT organization is built or destroyed
   466→through you. Every communication you make or fail to make is a data point
   467→in their assessment of whether the governance relationship is real or
   468→cosmetic. Make it real.

   472→8.  YOUR AUTHORITIES AND YOUR LIMITS
   473→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   474→
   475→What you are authorized to do:
   476→
   477→  – Create Demand Records on behalf of Service Customers
   478→  – Conduct demand assessment and determine outcome type (Redirect, Reject,
   479→    Quote) — subject to CM Leader approval on quotes and rejections
   480→  – Prepare quotes for CM Leader review and approval
   481→  – Record formal customer acceptance of quotes
   482→  – Submit ProcessHandover records to Change Enablement
   483→  – Maintain communication with Service Customers throughout demand lifecycle
   484→  – Identify catalog change needs and create Change Requests (with CM Leader
   485→    approval) for submission to CE
   486→  – Monitor SLA performance data for your Service Customers
   487→  – Issue governed SLA communications to your Service Customers
   488→  – Produce SLA performance reports (subject to CM Leader review before
   489→    issuance)
   490→
   491→What you are not authorized to do:
   492→
   493→  You cannot issue quotes without CM Leader approval.
   494→    Every quote is a commitment from the IT organization. You do not have
   495→    unilateral authority to make organizational commitments. The CM Leader
   496→    approval gate is structural — not a bureaucratic delay.
   497→
   498→  You cannot modify the service catalog directly.
   499→    Only Change Enablement may write to the catalog. This is the Separation
   500→    Principle (BR-CE-004). No matter how simple or obvious the catalog change
   501→    appears, it goes through CE.
   502→
   503→  You cannot communicate with Technical Owners directly about delivery.
   504→    Technical communication about change execution flows through Change
   505→    Enablement. You communicate with the CE Worker about progress. Not with
   506→    Technical Owners. Not with engineers.
   507→
   508→  You cannot make commitments on behalf of the CM Leader or Service Owner.
   509→    Your authority is to prepare, assess, and communicate. Governance decisions
   510→    at the commitment level require the approval of the people authorized to
   511→    make them.
   512→
   513→  You cannot serve Service Users in the CSM channel.
   514→    Service Users belong in the Service Desk. If a Service User contacts you,
   515→    redirect them appropriately. They are not your actor group.
   516→
   517→  You cannot also be the CE Leader for the same service scope.
   518→    Separation of duties: the person who assesses a demand and issues a quote
   519→    must not be the same person who approves the resulting change (DAL-16,
   520→    Section 7). This is structural — not a staffing preference.

   524→9.  YOUR RELATIONSHIP TO THE CM LEADER
   525→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   526→
   527→Your CM Leader is the operational authority for the entire CSM function. They
   528→hold accountability for throughput, assessment quality, and communication
   529→standards across all SCM Workers. They are not a supervisor in the traditional
   530→sense — they are the governance authority above you in the CSM function.
   531→
   532→What the CM Leader does for you:
   533→
   534→  Approves quotes before issuance
   535→    You prepare. They validate. No quote leaves CSM without their approval.
   536→
   537→  Approves rejection decisions where rejection is contested
   538→    If a Service Customer disputes a rejection, the CM Leader's authority is
   539→    the next governance level. You document; they authorize.
   540→
   541→  Escalates to the Service Owner when required
   542→    Commitment disputes, significant SLA breaches, and governance escalations
   543→    that go beyond the SCM Worker level move through the CM Leader to the
   544→    Service Owner.
   545→
   546→  Reviews SLA performance reports before they are issued
   547→    Your reports are accurate before the CM Leader sees them. Their review
   548→    ensures completeness and consistency before the Service Customer receives them.
   549→
   550→  Monitors the demand pipeline across all SCM Workers
   551→    The CM Leader has visibility into every active demand, its status, and its
   552→    communication health. If your demand is stalling — in UNDER_REVIEW too
   553→    long, IN_CHANGE without communication updates — the CM Leader will ask.
   554→
   555→What you do for the CM Leader:
   556→
   557→  You are the quality of CSM, made visible.
   558→    The CM Leader's accountability is yours in practice. Your assessment quality,
   559→    your communication discipline, your quote accuracy — these are what the
   560→    CM Leader is held accountable for. Poor discipline in your work is poor
   561→    discipline in the function they lead.
   562→
   563→  You surface problems early.
   564→    When a demand is going badly — when a change is delayed, when a Service
   565→    Customer is dissatisfied, when an SLA breach is likely — you tell the CM
   566→    Leader before it becomes a crisis. The CM Leader needs to know what is
   567→    happening before escalation lands on their desk from an external source.
   568→
   569→
   570→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   571→10. WORKING WITH CHANGE ENABLEMENT
   572→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   573→
   574→Change Enablement (CE) is your execution partner. You trigger changes. They
   575→execute them. You do not execute. They do not communicate with your Service
   576→Customers. This division of responsibilities is structural and mutual.
   577→
   578→Your obligations toward CE:
   579→
   580→  Submit complete, accurate ProcessHandover records
   581→    The CE Worker picks up your handover and begins assessment from what you
   582→    provide. A handover that is incomplete, ambiguous, or missing customer
   583→    context forces CE to start their work with a gap you created. The demand
   584→    record, the accepted quote scope, and the customer context must be in the
   585→    handover before you submit it.
   586→
   587→  Be available for CE queries during assessment and planning
   588→    CE may need to clarify scope or customer expectations before they can plan
   589→    the change. You are the channel to the Service Customer. Respond promptly.
   590→
   591→  Track CE progress and communicate to your Service Customer
   592→    CE reports milestones to you. You communicate them to the Service Customer.
   593→    If CE is delayed, you communicate that. If CE encounters unexpected
   594→    complexity, you communicate that. The Service Customer does not hear about
   595→    change execution from CE directly.
   596→
   597→  Verify the catalog upon CE completion
   598→    When CE confirms the change is complete, you verify the catalog entry
   599→    reflects the committed scope before closing the demand.
   600→
   601→CE's obligations toward you:
   602→
   603→  Acknowledge the ProcessHandover record (BR-HO-001)
   604→    CE assumes execution responsibility from this acknowledgement.
   605→
   606→  Report progress at defined milestones
   607→    CE communicates status to you — not to your Service Customer — at key
   608→    points in the change lifecycle.
   609→
   610→  Confirm completion
   611→    CE confirms to you when the change is complete and the catalog is updated.
   612→    This is your trigger to verify and confirm fulfillment to the Service
   613→    Customer.
   614→
   615→If CE and CSM disagree about scope or delivery, the resolution path is:
   616→SCM Worker ↔ CE Worker → CM Leader ↔ CE Leader → Service Owner (if required).
   617→
   618→
   619→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   620→11. WORKING WITH PROBLEM MANAGEMENT AND SLA BREACHES
   621→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   622→
   623→You are not a participant in Problem Management. You are a recipient of its
   624→outputs — specifically when Problem Management's findings affect SLA commitments
   625→for your Service Customers.
   626→
   627→What Problem Management produces that is relevant to you:
   628→
   629→  Root cause findings for SLA breach patterns
   630→    When Problem Management completes its investigation into a recurring
   631→    failure, the findings — root cause, decision (fix / Known Error / accept),
   632→    and remediation path — feed into your SLA governance communication. When
   633→    you communicate an SLA breach to your Service Customer, you provide this
   634→    context. Not just "there was a breach," but "here is what was investigated,
   635→    here is the decision, and here is what it means for the service going forward."
   636→
   637→  Change triggers that affect your catalog
   638→    When Problem Management decides a structural fix is warranted, it triggers
   639→    a change in Change Enablement. That change may affect catalog entries in
   640→    your customers' portfolios. You communicate this — before the change
   641→    executes if possible, certainly after.
   642→
   643→  Known Error resolutions
   644→    When a Known Error is resolved (the underlying problem is fixed via a
   645→    change), the Known Error record is closed. If the Known Error was associated
   646→    with a service in your customer's portfolio, you communicate the resolution:
   647→    the issue is structurally fixed, not just worked around.
   648→
   649→Your role in breach communication is irreplaceable:
   650→  Problem Management produces the root cause and decision.
   651→  Service Level Management produces the performance data.
   652→  You translate both into a customer communication that is honest, timely,
   653→  and governed. A breach that reaches your Service Customer through an
   654→  automated system notification, without context or human communication from
   655→  you, is a governance failure in the customer relationship. Not a process
   656→  step. A relationship failure.

   660→12. COGNI: YOUR OPERATIONAL INTELLIGENCE
   661→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   662→
   663→Cogni is available to you as an operational intelligence layer within the
   664→Experience Layer. It is governed by the same entitlement boundaries as
   665→everything else — your view is scoped to your Service Customer assignments.
   666→
   667→What Cogni does for you as an SCM Worker:
   668→
   669→  Smart Stage
   670→    – Demand estimation assistance: based on historical demand data for similar
   671→      requests, Cogni surfaces estimated effort ranges for your assessment.
   672→      This is advisory — your judgment governs the quote. Cogni reduces
   673→      the lookup burden, not the governance responsibility.
   674→    – SLA performance at a glance: a dashboard of SLA status across your
   675→      Service Customers, surfacing warnings and breach risks before you would
   676→      otherwise notice them.
   677→    – Communication drafting: after an SLA breach event, Cogni drafts a
   678→      structured customer communication incorporating Problem Management root
   679→      cause data — for you to review, adjust, and send. You do not send
   680→      AI-generated communications without review. Accountability is yours.
   681→    – Demand pipeline visibility: status of all active Demand Records assigned
   682→      to you, with time-in-state flags for records that may be stalling.
   683→
   684→  Mature Stage
   685→    – Demand forecasting: based on your Service Customers' usage patterns and
   686→      demand history, Cogni identifies likely upcoming demands — allowing you
   687→      to be proactive rather than reactive.
   688→    – SLA class recommendation: when monitoring data suggests a service's
   689→      actual criticality has shifted, Cogni surfaces the analysis for your
   690→      discussion with the customer and the CM Leader.
   691→
   692→What Cogni never does:
   693→  – Issue quotes or make commitments on your behalf
   694→  – Communicate with Service Customers without your review
   695→  – Surface another customer's data within your view
   696→  – Replace the CM Leader approval step for quotes

   699→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   700→13. WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE
   701→────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   702→
   703→An SCM Worker is performing their role well when:
   704→
   705→  Every active demand has a documented, current status.
   706→    No demand sits in UNDER_REVIEW or IN_CHANGE without a recent communication
   707→    record. No demand is open past fulfillment.
   708→
   709→  Quotes are accurate.
   710→    The IT organization delivers what it quoted. When scope must change, the
   711→    Service Customer is told before the change happens, not after.
   712→
   713→  Communication is ahead of the Service Customer.
   714→    Your customers do not contact you to ask for status. You have already told
   715→    them. This is not a matter of sending more messages — it is a matter of
   716→    timing communication at the right moments, with the right content.
   717→
   718→  SLA warnings are never silent.
   719→    Every SLAWarning event for your customers results in a proactive
   720→    communication from you. Before the breach.
   721→
   722→  Breach communications contain context.
   723→    When a breach occurs, your Service Customer understands what happened, what
   724→    is being done, and what it means for their services. Not just a timestamp.
   725→
   726→  Your catalog entries reflect current reality.
   727→    When you verify a catalog entry after a change, you actually verify it.
   728→    Discrepancies are returned to CE for correction before you close the loop.
   729→
   730→  You surface problems to your CM Leader before they become external issues.
   731→    The CM Leader never hears about a demand problem from the Service Customer
   732→    before hearing it from you.
   733→
   734→The standard is not perfection. The standard is governed accountability.
   735→Problems happen. What distinguishes a well-run CSM function is not the absence
   736→of problems — it is the presence of governance when problems occur.
